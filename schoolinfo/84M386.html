Success Academy Harlem 4 Charter School

Grades K-4

Our Insights

What’s Special

Member of high-performing Success Charter network

The Downside

Space constraints in building shared with two other schools

Success Academy Harlem 4 is the fourth in New York’s provocative Success Academy charter network that has quickly grown to 12 elementary and two middle schools. Harlem 4 has hallmarks of many Success Academy schools: uniforms, firm discipline, longer school days, non-union faculty, a shared campus, a focus on student achievement, and an annual lottery to determine which students earn admission. The school has become a sought-after choice for many Harlem residents unhappy with traditional public schools: In 2013, the school received 2,121 application for 97 available kindergarten seats.

Danique Day Loving has been principal of Harlem 4 since September 2010, when she replaced Mitch Center. A Harlem native, Loving had previously been a teacher at Success Academy Harlem 2, and she seems to have a warm bond with the students who see her as a role model. [Day Loving moved to Success Academy Harlem 1 in 2013 and was replaced by William Loskoch. Loskoch was a "Leadership Resident" at Success Academy between 2011 and 2013.]

Harlem 4 opened in 2008 and in 2009 moved to its present campus, a 1937 school building that it shares with PS 241 and Opportunity Charter (a combined middle and high school). The three schools share the auditorium, gym and play room. Loving describes the schools’ relationships as “cordial,” although advocates for PS 241 and Opportunity have complained Success Academy is squeezing them out of their home.

As at other Success Academy elementary schools, Harlem 4’s hallway floors are dotted with numbers and words (so even class transitions can be instructional) and classroom rugs contain circles so kids know precisely where to sit. Children dress in the network’s uniform, which features orange ties for boys and plaid jumpers for girls.

School days begin promptly at 7:45 a.m. A fixed schedule moves kids briskly from one lesson to the next, and timers keep the day on pace. Often, a teacher will explain an assignment, then kids will obediently break into small groups to do the work as a timer ticks off the seconds. Teachers often use the “turn and talk” technique, in which pairs of students discuss or review what has just been learned.

Each day, Harlem 4 students get a science lesson that includes a 20-minute hands-on experiment, part of the “discovery-based” method of science education. On our visit, 1st-graders were eagerly learning to measure and pour precise amounts of liquid in a calibrated beaker. Displayed on the wall were sealed plastic “eco-columns” filled with plants that had been turned into a self-sustaining biosphere.

Harlem 4’s math instruction (like all Success Academy elementaries) follows the TERC Investigations curriculum, supplemented by other programs. A “constructivist” approach lets students use a variety of strategies to solve math problems. Kids have recess and snack daily and regularly get PE, art and music classes, but no foreign language.

Young kids learn to read and write using the phonics-based Success For All reading program, as well as the THINK Literacy program developed by Success Academy experts. During reading lessons, students frequently break into small groups of kids who are at the same reading level, and it’s not uncommon for a group to be joined by a child from another grade who is reading above or below grade level. We observed 1st-graders being quizzed about the poem “You Oughta Meet Danitra Brown.” Teachers followed up student answers with questions such as “How do you know that?” and “What’s your evidence?”

Children moved silently in “zero noise” halls and move efficiently from one lesson to the next. Teachers often grab students’ attention with sing-song statements like, “All eyes on Andrew because what he has to say is …” which elicits a loud group cry of “IMPORTANT!” Correct answers to questions may earn a small sticker (many were visible on collars or foreheads) or a shout-out from fellow students that goes “My sister, my sister, my sister’s ON FIRE!”

Despite this, Success Academy schools are famous for a no-nonsense attitude toward bad behavior. Defiant kids who don’t obey school rules (which are posted conspicuously in classrooms and halls) quickly earn punishments ranging from brief timeouts to school suspensions.

The school demands a great deal from its teachers, who typically join the Success Academy system right out of college (and who then spend a year in training under the wing of an established teacher). Harlem 4’s non-union teachers don’t have the job protections common at traditional public schools and can be fired if administrators feel they aren’t doing a good job. Teachers must freely give out their cell phone numbers and be available at all hours to answer parents’ questions. Roughly half Harlem 4’s teachers have fewer than four years teaching experience.

Success Academy network administrators handle day-to-day bureaucratic chores, freeing on-site educators to focus on instruction. Network leaders also make all major decisions about curriculum, textbooks and methods, which often leaves faculty little say in what or how subjects are taught. Some teachers bristle at the centralized planning; in the 2011-2012 DOE Learning Environment Survey, 58 percent of teachers disagreed with the statement, “In my school, it’s easy to speak up about what’s on your mind.” But Harlem 4’s teachers generally give principal Loving high marks, and a large majority say they would recommend the school to parents seeking a place for a child.

When we visited in 2013, Harlem 4’s two classes of 5th-graders were being taught six blocks north in classrooms at Harlem 1, the latest in a series of location changes required as the Success Academy schools expand beyond the capacity of their shared spaces. (“They’ve moved more than any kids in the city,” said Jenny Sedlis, the Success Academy network’s director of external affairs.)

Special education: Children with special needs are taught in Integrated Co-Teaching (ICT) classes that feature two teachers, one of whom is certified to teach special education.

Admissions: Lottery, with priority to children living in District 3. (Skip Card, June 2013)
