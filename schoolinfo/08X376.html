Our Insights

What’s Special

Middle school kids get lockers

The Downside

Working to improve discipline and order

The vision: Start thinking about college in 6th grade.

The reality: Founded in collaboration with the College Board (the organization that sponsors the SATs), this small school in the Adlai Stevenson Campus serves kids in grades 6 through 12. The small size--and the fact that it serves both middle school and high school--is both a plus and a minus: kids gets lots of attention and sometimes have the same teachers in high school that they had in middle school, providing lots of continuity. But some kids find it a bit claustrophobic and leave after 8th grade for other high schools, sometimes in the same building.

There are some nice extras. The middle school kids feel grown up because they have their own lockers. There's an after-school program sponsored by the YMCA with homework help and fun activities like bicycling. And kids get to go to on an overnight trip to a YMCA camp in New Jersey. Kids who don't want to face the cafeteria can take their lunch in library. Attendance is good and kids mostly seemed happy and engaged in the classes we visited.

However, there are some significant issues. The founding principal was never able to gain the trust of teachers, and one-third of the teachers responding to the Learning Environment survey said order and discipline are not maintained.

Principal Jung-Hyun Kym took the helm in 2014. She was an assistant principal at Pathways in Technology Early College High School.

The school opened with a 6th grade in 2006 and is now a 6-12th grade school with approximately 470 students.

Special education: Nearly half the classes serve children with special needs, either in self-contained rooms or team-taught classrooms with a mix of general ed and special ed kids.

Admissions: District 8 school choice for middle school, limited unscreened for high school. (Clara Hemphill, January 2011; Lydie Raschka, school data, October 2014)
