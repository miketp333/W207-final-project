I.S. 73 The Frank Sansivieri Intermediate School

Grades 6-8

Our Insights

What’s Special

Impressive art program; reduced class sizes for language arts and math instruction

The Downside

Limited foreign language instruction; academic performance needs to improve

"I don't forget science," Principal Frank Mifsud said as we toured a large, well-equipped science lab at IS 73. Indeed it would be surprising if he did -- Mifsud started out as a science teacher at the school in 1975. Reflecting on his tenure of three decades (he became principal in 1998), Mifsud cited the delivery of instruction as one of the biggest changes he has witnessed. "The days of a teacher standing in front of a classroom just relying on a lesson plan are over," he said. "When I walk into a silent room, I expect there to be a test. I like to hear chatter in classrooms, as long as it is accountable talk."

On the day of our visit we observed a relaxed environment where students, who don't wear uniforms, feel at ease addressing faculty and administrators in the hallways. In one special education class, the students had returned to their room before the end of lunch for the comfort of being near their teacher -- something the teacher encouraged even though it cut into her lunch break. Though hallway traffic between periods was noisy and hectic, we didn't notice too many stragglers after the class bell rang.

The school follows the America's Choice teaching approach, which is built on the premise that clear standards and structure are the best antidote to low performance. Each class starts with a teacher-guided mini-lesson followed by small-group and individual work. We observed this format in action in an 8th grade language arts class where students were wrapping up a mini-journalism lesson on framing an article. In a social studies class, students were engaged in small-group discussions after completing an independent reading assignment on the African slave trade.

Although there is plenty of room for improvement in standardized test scores across all grades, the school has had some successes in recent years. Scores have risen, and in 2004 approximately 50 graduates were accepted to one of the city's specialized high schools.

To bolster performance, the school offers reduced class sizes for language arts and math instruction. "Ramp Up," an academic enrichment program originally designed for at-risk high school students, is used on top of the basic curriculum to help high achieving bilingual students move towards more sophisticated levels of literacy. In addition to technology and music, there is a strong art program as evidenced by the impressive talent on display in the school's spacious art studios. One casualty of the mounting emphasis on standardized test performance, however, is foreign language instruction. Because of the increased time and funding allotted for core subjects, languages are not introduced until the 8th grade for most students, and the 7th grade for those in the special progress ("SP") track for high achieving students.

In 2004, the school was reorganized into five separate academies in an effort to provide greater oversight. Each academy is assigned its own assistant principal, dean, and guidance counselor and incorporates students across the 6th, 7th, and 8th grades. Four of the five are identical in structure, offering students the same general education curriculum. Each academy also has its own SP track. The fifth one is dedicated to the school's ELL (English Language Learners) students, but beginning with the 2005-06 school year, the school plans to make all five academies the same, incorporating ELL students into each one.

The school's main building was erected in 1928. Two wings were added later on: one in 1969 and the other in 1989. There are two gyms, a large library, an auditorium, three computer labs, and a cafeteria.

English as a Second Language: The school has bilingual classes in every grade and offers English-as-a-Second-Language support services to students individually and in small groups.

Special education: There are "self-contained" classes, only for children with special needs, in all grades. In addition, each grade has at least one team-taught "inclusion" class where two teachers work in the same classroom with both general education students and those needing special services.

After school: The school offers programs operated by independent organizations. (Laura Zingmond, November 2004)

Note: Principal Frank Mifsud, whom we met on our tour, retired in June 2005.
