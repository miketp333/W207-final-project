The Downside

Very needy student population

Hunts Point has undergone three name changes in just over a decade, a sure sign of a school that has struggled. Many children enter with special needs and very low skills. Disciplinary action has resulted in high numbers of student suspensions. However, a new principal has hired a full-time dean to help with discipline. That, along with a character education program, has helped decrease suspensions and improve attendance.

Sonya Johnson took the helm in 2012. She was the assistant principal under former principal John Hughes. Prior to that, she worked as a high school English teacher at Frederick Douglass Academy and as a literacy coach at PS 66. She has made it a priority to hire good teachers and to give them professional development in order to strengthen academics. According to the 2013 Quality Review, lessons tend to be one-size-fits-all, rather than aimed at different abilities. Test scores show that many students do not know their number facts and have limited vocabulary.

Under the name PS/IS 74, Hunts Point lingered for many years on the state's list of failing schools and was nicknamed "the jungle.” In 2000 it was reorganized into MS 201, a magnet school focused on the arts, but landed again on the city’s failing list four years in a row. In 2008 half the teaching staff was let go and the school reopened as Hunts Point.

According to an article in the New York Sun, the school had become a “dumping ground” for unusually high numbers of children with disabilities shut out by more selective area schools.

The building is currently shared with Hyde Leadership Charter School, and PS 352, a District 75 program for children with serious special needs. Despite the publicity, Hunts Point still has higher numbers of children with special needs. Hyde only has 13.2 %, while Hunts has 29 percent. The schools share a renovated playground, an auditorium, a gym, a cafeteria and a dance studio. All students must pass through a metal detector upon entry. The building has some stuffy windowless classrooms and pockets of clutter in hallways and classrooms.

However, Johnson maintains a well-organized website with calendars of important dates, a countdown to state exams and pages outlining expectations, as well as parent and teacher resources. The day begins with the Pledge of Allegiance. Uniforms consist of green pants and green, tan or white crewneck t-shirts. If students do not complete assignments and homework they report to a special learning area to make up work, an initiative known as ZAP, or Zeros Aren’t Permitted.

The school has a Mouse Squad, a group of students in charge of keeping the technology in working order. English language learners use self-paced computer programs for some lessons. Higher performing students may take algebra and attend an English enrichment program after school.

Several community organizations have offices at the school: South Bronx Mental Health, Urban Health League, Pathways for Youth, and the Liberty Project. These organizations provide services to students at the school and Pathways runs an after-school program with tutoring.

Special education: There are "self-contained" classes with small numbers of only children with special needs. There are also Integrated Co-Teaching (ICT) classes, in which students with special needs learn side-by-side with their general education peers, and two teachers, one of whom is trained to teach special education.

Admissions: Neighborhood school (Lydie Raschka, April 2013)
