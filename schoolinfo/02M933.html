The Downside

Too soon to tell

The arts-focused City Knoll Middle School MS 933 opened in midtown Manhattan in September 2014 with the promise of helping to alleviate congestion in overcrowded District 2. In its first year, it shared a building with PS 51 and in 2015 it moved to a former Catholic school building onWest 33rd Streetwhich which had been occupied by Clinton School for Writers and Artists.

Before conceiving the idea for City Knoll, founding principal Victoria Armas was a teacher and then worked for the Department of Education doing quality reviews of schools. Armas was featured as "Principal of the Week" by DNAinfo soon after the school opened. Armas told DNAinfo that founding teachers were selected because they were all "really smart" and had a connection to the arts.

The school curriculum is strongly tied to the Common Core Standards. "It's definitely more rigorous," Armas told DNAinfo. "But I feel that [for] our youngsters to be able to compete in the global society that we live in [they] need to be exposed to these kinds of standards." The school uses Connected Mathematics Project for math and Expeditionary Learning for English language arts, a curriculum known for its emphasis on hands-on learning and field trips.

Student projects encourage inquiry, research and reflection and are interdisciplinary. Students visited the High Line as part of a study of public spaces. Science and math classes examined the High Line's construction, while history classes studied important public spaces around the world.

Everyone at the school does three minutes of mindful breathing at various intervals throughout the day. Students get mentoring and advising as well as academic enrichment and intervention in an "exchange" program. They have time every week to conduct independent study and research on topics of their choice.

City Knoll has partnerships with the Museum of Modern Art, the Paley Center for Media, The Educational Video Center, NYC Outward Bound and the YMCA. According to the school website, these partnerships provide students access to additional resources and opportunities and support the arts-focused, project-based school philosophy.

Special Education: According to the middle school handbook, City Knoll provides students with the services that are indicated on their IEPs (individualized education programs).

Admissions: Limited unscreened District 2, with preference given to students who sign up at an admissions event. (Ella Colley, school website and news reports, February 2015)[Photo from www.cityknollms.org]
