Our Insights

What’s Special

Large arts program, numerous extra-curricular programs.

The Downside

Overcrowding, temporary buildings.

MAY 2014 UPDATE: Starting in the fall of 2014, IS 125 began serving grades 6-8, rather than 5-8. 

OCTOBER 2004 REVIEW: Despite overcrowding and underfunding, IS 125 manages to maintain a rich arts program, numerous extra-curricular activities, and strong support for students learning to speak English. More than 1,700 students attend classes on its six-building campus, a size that at many other schools would result in individual students getting lost in the multitudes. At IS 125, however, a dedicated and caring faculty and staff work hard to provide personalized instruction.

In response to the school's growing enrollment years ago, the city constructed a temporary mini-building in 1991. Designers intended for it to be used only for up to 10 years, but 13 years later, the "temporary" building houses 21 classes in small rooms with walls too unsturdy to support bookshelves. Climate control is a "constant issue," said Principal Judy Lynn Mittler. Rooms are cold in the winter and too warm in the summer.

Four smaller makeshift buildings, with two classes each, have gone up as well since 1991. Mittler said that she would like to see a new facility or permanent addition to the present building, which was erected in 1924, but the Department of Education is currently focused on constructing high schools.

In 2004, the school was divided into four "houses," each with its own dean, counselor, and students of all grades. Most classes meet in one area of the campus and are taught by a designated faculty. The purpose, Mittler said, is to offer a more intimate setting, allowing for lasting bonds to be created among peers, faculty, and staff. Students spend all four years within their houses.

Most classes contain 25 to 30 pupils. Physical education teachers, however, often teach classes three times as large, in order to serve the entire population. The gym program is not co-educational; we watched more than 60 female students convene in a half-size gym.

Although most instructors teach all day in their own rooms, some do a lot of running. Of the school's 123 teachers, 22 must teach in multiple locations. "I'm in 15 rooms," said a science teacher. "It's hard, because I have lots of equipment. It's a challenge, but I've done it before. And the kids are very adaptive."

Most of IS 125's students are Hispanic, and Mittler estimates that more than half of these kids do not speak English at home. The school's 11-member English Language Learner faculty serves 450 students, and most regular classroom teachers are bilingual. We saw an 8th grade class in which students were asked to describe an experiment in their native language, Spanish, then in English. Some gym classes are structured so that bilingual students act as translators for classmates.

The attention given to students learning English seems to have borne fruit. The percentage of 8th graders meeting standards on state literacy tests rose from 38 to 47 percent between 2003 and 2004. Sixth grade literacy scores rose five points to 38 percent.

Even though it has inadequate funding, IS 125 manages to stay rooted in the arts. Only vocal music had to be dropped this year. The orchestra, concert band, dance classes, and visual arts department participate in two annual festivals at the school. We saw a strings teacher teach a lesson in hand technique to a room full of violinists, cellists, and bassists. Beginners and more advanced musicians play together in heterogeneous ensembles, according to the concert band teacher. "Advanced players help show beginners what is possible," he said. Usually, only advanced students perform in concerts. The school has a large complement of orchestra and band instruments, which students can borrow -- a boon for those who cannot afford to rent or buy.

After school: Extra-curricular activities abound. Students can join law, photography, Kiwanis, and robotics clubs, or play on girls' and boys' basketball teams. The Champion's Club meets for about 90 minutes of tutoring before members participate in sports, dance, computers, karate and other activities. IS 125 also plans to offer extra-help classes in math and English. (John Thomas, October 2004)
