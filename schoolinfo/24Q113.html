Our Insights

What’s Special

Top-notch academics.

The Downside

No gym, and very overcrowded.

July 2012: Longtime principal Anthony Pranzo passed away on April 19, 2012. The former assistant principal Alejandro Megias became principal.

Located in a pleasant neighborhood of tree-lined streets, PS 113 ranks among the city's top-performers on standardized tests. It is also a stable and orderly school, run for close to two decades by Principal Anthony Pranzo, an administrator both liked and respected by his faculty. The school has long taken a traditional approach to teaching, but that is changing under some pressure from the city Department of Education, which encourages more progressive approaches. Our impression was that the transition is going smoothly, and we noticed that in one important measure of the progressive approachstudent group-workthe children seemed well behaved and focused.

Pranzo gets high marks from his teachers. Every staff member we spoke to during our visit described him as a supportive leader who rolls up his sleeves and get things done. Not one to hide in his office, Pranzo spends a lot of time visiting classrooms. He is also, apparently, thick-skinned; Pranzo told us that someone other than himself would give us our school tour, so that teachers would feel free to speak openly. One teacher we met contrasted Pranzo's leadership with that of the principal in her last school, saying that being at PS 113 was akin to having "died and gone to heaven."

Since 2003, the Department of Education has required all but about 200 schools in the city to adhere to a curriculum that centers on progressive approacheschildren reading literature that they choose rather than readers, for example. PS 113 could have stuck to its traditional approaches, but like many "exempt" schools, it has chosen to adopt a number of the education department's preferred methods. That means at least part of the faculty is being urged to make changes it would prefer not to. Nonetheless, the mood at the school during our visit was upbeat. Staff morale was high, and teachers maintained a good rapport with students.

We saw lots of student art and writing posted in the classrooms and in the halls, which were filled with bulletin boards. If the examples displayed are any indication, the school does a good job of teaching writing skills. There is also a lot of time devoted to test prep during standardized test seasonup to 75 percent of the school day, to the dismay of one parent we spoke to. Teachers defend the practice, however, saying tests weigh heavily in a child's future, and that the faculty works real education, not just drill, into the prep.

One unusual offering at PS 113 is an international dance program, which teaches each grade dances of a different nationality. The school ends the year with a program of performances as well as an international food festival to which parents contribute.

PS 113's weak spot is a serious space shortage, which is getting worse every year, according to the parent coordinator. There is no room for a science lab, for example, so the science teacher rolls carts with equipment from classroom to classroom, and there is no gym or library. Every corner of the building, including hallways, is put to use as class space, allowing class sizes to remain under 30. This means that school staff, including the parent coordinator and the guidance counselor, must work out of converted bathrooms and closets. The school does house a computer lab, however, and recently received new computers for it.

The school benefits from very active parent participation, according to the parent coordinator. The PTA plans events and trips for students and actively tries to get resources for teachers; it bought a screen so the computer teacher could project demonstrations onto the wall, for example.

Special education: Resources are limited, a drawback to parents of children with special needs. (Tom Huser, January 2005; updated Philissa Cramer, March 2006. This school is featured in New York City's Best Public Elementary Schools.)
