Our Insights

What’s Special

Lovely building and a gentle, relaxed atmosphere

The Downside

Crowded classrooms; emphasis on project-based work may not be for everyone

IS 289 is an unusually calm and gentle school, where children work together on ambitious, creative projects that often incorporate a social justice theme. Students call teachers by their first names and learn to design their own homework assignments. Although classes are large, teachers are attentive and the 6th graders get special attention as they learn how to develop the skills needed to do the multi-step projects favored in all classes.

IS 289, which shares a building with PS 89, was built by the Battery Park City Authority in 1998. The elementary school serves the neighborhood children, but the middle school is open to students from all of District 2. One of the most attractive physical plants in the city, the spacious hallways have nooks and steps, but this is at the expense of space in the classrooms, which feels crowded with up to 33 children per room.

In some ways this middle school feels more like a college the way teens kick back with their laptops and confer and jot things down in one of the many notebooks they use instead of textbooks. In Spanish class, their projects may take the form of travel brochures or a mock-up of a Facebook page; in math, after drawing and calculating the volume of three containers, students build the containers out of cardstock. Teachers value choice as part of the learning process so when 8th graders study immigration, they may choose the form a project will take, whether it's a film or a paper or letters. According to students, the projects are hard work, especially when they have more than one going at a time. The results we saw were attractive and academically rigorous.

At the end of the year, the entire 7th grade collaborates on an opera as a culmination of their work in U.S. History. One year it was "Salem Supernaturals" based on the Salem witch trials. Students write the script, build the sets, create the lighting and design the publicity.

Families looking for "a lot of tests and five-paragraph essay work, more traditional assignments," will be disappointed, according to longtime Principal Ellen Foote. Workshops help orient and educate parents to this way of working. Sixth graders get extra help in learning how to collaborate, plan and prepare. Sixth grade teachers work with a co-teacher during homeroom to help students stay on track. "We know how important structure is," said Foote. Every child has a planner and subjects are color-coded. "I'm checking your work as you walk out the door with your planner," we heard a humanities teacher say. [Ellen Foote retired in 2013. Her successor is former assistant principal Zeynep Ozkan, who holds a degree in biology from Boston College and a master's degree in education from Harvard University.]

Special education: Some children are pulled out for individual and small group instruction and there is an Integrated Co-Teaching (ICT) class on each grade taught by two teachers, one of whom is trained in special education. The "self-contained" special education class is just as inviting, cheery and well-equipped as any in the building and these children seem fully integrated into the life of the school. On our visit they were preparing a play about Ancient Greek gods that they wrote themselves. "They are just as, if not more capable than their [general education] peers," said the teacher. IS 289 uses the Connected Math curriculum, an approach that encourages problem solving rather than memorizing formulas, and there is an algebra class for advanced math students.

Afterschool: Students praised the after school offerings, which include study lab, basketball, track, softball, baseball, volleyball, wresting and instrumental music.

High school choices: For high school, students attend an array of schools including Quest to Learn, Stuyvesant, Brooklyn Tech, Bronx Science, LaGuardia and School of the Future.

Admissions: The administration is committed to having a range of academic abilities, a mix of children from different ethnic groups and income levels and a geographic distribution of children within District 2. Prospective students participate in a lesson in that has a whole group, small group and independent component. (Lydie Raschka, May 2013; principal update December 2014)
