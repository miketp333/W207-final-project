Our Insights

What’s Special

Exceptional arts facilities and programs.

The Downside

No physical education teacher; lack of parental involvement.

MARCH 2007 UPDATE: Carol Uffer is no longer the school's principal. The principal is now Anne Alfonso. The school also nowincludes grades 6-8.

OCTOBER 2004 VISIT: PS 164 suffers from declining enrollment, not because it is undesirable, but because it has been overlooked. It boasts a progressive, veteran faculty; a principal whose commitment to literacy has raised test scores; and arts facilities that rival most high schools. Nestled in an affluent, predominantly Orthodox Jewish neighborhood, the school imports most of its children from outside the immediate area, because many local families send their kids to private religious schools.

In 2003, enrollment at the school, where children wear uniforms, slid from 485 to 440 students. To boost numbers, Principal Carol Uffer who was an early childhood specialist for eight years expanded kindergarten by arranging with regional administrators to bring in more children with special needs. Kindergarten grew from two to five sections: two "self-contained" classes for children with special needs only (one is bilingual Spanish), two general education classes, and one team-taught "inclusion" class for both general education students and students with special needs.

Uffer has focused on literacy. The library and classrooms overflow with books, and the teachers' reading supplies room is lined with additional materials, color-coded by difficulty level. Classes have meeting rugs where teachers share their own writing, urging students to compose essays on topics that inspire them. Even instructors of special subjects teach literacy. While encouraging his charges to stretch and meditate, the dance teacher referred to themes contained in A Quiet Place, PS 164's book of the month.

Test scores are rising. Last year, the number of 4th graders meeting standards on the state English Language Arts test jumped from 58 to 74 percent. Math scores have remained equally high. Uffer plans to bolster scores further with after-school test preparation in the spring. She gives credit for improved scores to her veteran faculty; only six teachers have less than five years of teaching experience. Some, however, feel overwhelmed by the new teaching strategies that the city began to require in most schools in 2003. "Not as much training was given to out-of-classroom teachers," said English-as-a-Second-Language (ESL) instructor Rose Lewinson, who struggles to stay abreast of new methods while contending with an onerous student load.

Fourteen years ago, the Board of Education designated PS 164 an arts magnet school, and an emphasis on the arts is still evident, even though the school no longer has magnet status. A former PS 164 student, singer-songwriter Paul Simon, would probably be impressed with its dance studio, drama room, and video production studio. Kindergartners and 1st graders learn to play piano in "Music and the Brain," a two-year course designed to enhance academic achievement through music. Third- and fourth-graders rotate through instrumental music, vocal music, dance, visual arts, and video production electives. In grades five and six, they focus on one discipline.

Teachers vote on funding for special subjects. For 2004 they reinstated a science teacher and eliminated physical education. Exercise now comes from recess. The New York City Council gave the school a grant to update its computer lab with 32 new Macintosh computers.

Marie Rivera, Parent Association co-president, calls the schools' parents "commitment-phobic," and there is a noticeable absence of parents in the building. Almost 70 percent of school families do not speak English at home, said Uffer, who believes the lack of involvement stems from cultural barriers. To bridge this gap she supplies interpreters at parent meetings and school functions. Lucille Atkinson, the school's parent coordinator, has increased involvement through Learning Leaders, a citywide program that trains parent volunteers as tutors. Despite the lack of involvement, the three-member Parent Association has managed to raise enough money to purchase a new audio system and projector for the auditorium and refurbish equipment in the video studio. Each year the PA also buys "wish list" items for teachers. The school could further boost involvement by displaying more student work on classroom and hallway walls. As more and more parents are enticed into the school, their attention would be drawn to these displays and the fine work being done in PS 164's classrooms.

Special education: In addition to the kindergarten classes, there are 5th and 6th grade self-contained classes, plus a 6th grade inclusion class. (This school is featured in New York City's Best Public Elementary Schools. John Thomas, October 2004)
