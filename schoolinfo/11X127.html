Our Insights

What’s Special

Students tackle problems in their community and do lots of research and writing

The Downside

Some classes engage students more than others

MS 127 is a neighborhood middle school that draws students from a diverse patch of the Parkchester area in the Bronx. It serves students of all skill levels, ranging from struggling learners to high achievers. It is home to a districtwide honors program and offers a lot of support for its many English language learners, who mostly are native Spanish or Bengali speakers.

The school benefits from the steady leadership of its longtime principal, Harry Sherman. Since his arrival in 2005 Sherman has hired roughly two-thirds of the schools current staff, which includes a mix of young and more experienced teachers. Newer teachers are well-supported and are paired with a more experienced colleague who serves as their mentor.

The vibe throughout the school is calm and orderly, at least partly due to the fact that students dont pour into the hallways in between classes. Rather, students stay in the same classroom for most of the day while their teachers travel to them.

The third floor hallway, where most of the schools classrooms are located, is lined with student essays. MS 127 shares the building with The Linden Tree Elementary School and some lower grade classes from nearby PS 106.

There is one honors class on each grade that follows the same curriculum as the rest of the school. In honors classes, teachers may spend less time on fundamental skills and infuse more challenging assignments into daily lessons, but otherwise instruction follows the same calendar and units of study as the rest of the school.

In classrooms we observed a range of teaching styles and students engaged to varying degrees. For instance, in one 6th-grade math class, students enthusiastically worked in groups as they brainstormed how to create a board game using geometric concepts, such as area, perimeter and volume.

Its not boring, one student said about the math class, adding that many assignments involve similar hands-on, collaborative work.

Elsewhere, students in a class for English language learners created comic strips tackling the controversial subject of whether 19th-century slave revolt leader Nat Turner was a madman or a hero. In another math class, students offered lengthy critiques of a fictitious students attempt at simplifying an algebraic expression.

In contrast, quiet, independent work seemed to dominate science instruction. In one class, students quietly unscrambled weather-related terms. Its just a worksheet to see how well we understood the packet, one student explained. Elsewhere, 7th-graders took notes as they watched a series of slides on blood cells projected on a large screen.

In the 8th grade, stronger students take high school Regents courses in algebra and either living environment or Earth science.

All students complete challenging, end-of-year projects through Participatory Action Research (PAR), a program developed by the Institute for Urban and Minority Education at Teachers College, Columbia University. Each grade tackles a different problem faced by their community, such as gang violence or varying images of beauty. Students are expected to research their community problem, identify solutions and present their findings through a research paper and PowerPoint presentation.

Teachers and staff encourage students to develop long-term goals. Students visit local colleges including Shermans alma mater, St. Johns University. Students in the bilingual instruction classes visit a local airport to learn about jobs that require knowledge of multiple languages.

In addition to core academic courses, students take elective classes that vary by grade, but include visual arts, music and theater production, and technology.

Each year typically 15 to 20 students are accepted to specialized high schools, according to Sherman. Most students, however, attend Lehman or a range of small high schools throughout the Bronx.

Afterschool activities include a range of boys', girls' and coed sports teams and clubs, including dance, step and karate.

Special education: There are self-contained classes for students with special needs only, integrated co-teaching (ICT) classes and SETSS. The school offers bilingual instruction classes for native Spanish and Bengali speakers.

Admissions: The school admits students zoned for the building and also admits students districtwide. For District 11 students not zoned for MS 127, priority goes to those who demonstrate interest by signing in at a school tour, open house, information session or middle school fair. Admission to the honors program is based on student attendance and performance on the New York State math and English language arts exams.(Jamaal Abdul-Alim and Laura Zingmond, May, 2014).
