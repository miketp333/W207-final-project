Our Insights

What’s Special

Spacious building, community service programs

The Downside

Shaky first few years

Bedford Stuyvesant New Beginnings Charter School (BSNBCS) opened in 2010, aiming to provide a strong academic environment for elementary and middle school students in low-performing District 16. The school offers traditional instruction and high expectations mixed with hands-on learning opportunities, including a productive indoor urban farm.

In its first few years, BSNBCS had high teacher turnover and school surveys showed that parents and teachers were dissatisfied: Fewer than 60 percent of teachers in the 2013-2014 school survey said they would recommend the school to other parents. There was a shake-up of leadership in 2014 and progressive educator Nicholas Tishuk became executive director. A former English teacher, Tishuk and his wife Rita co-founded the Renaissance Charter High School for Innovation in Harlem. This very progressive high school is a contrast to BSNBCS, where students wear uniforms and observe strict rules, such as walking in neat lines between classes and maintaining silence in hallways. Tishuk has lived in Bed-Stuy for over a decade and is strongly committed to the local community. "We want to find the kids who otherwise wouldn't be getting great education and work with them," Tishuk said, "We see this as a place where kids can be safe, where they can learn and grow into the young people they are going to be."

The first challenge is to get kids to come to school. The school has high chronic absenteeism at 37 percent (meaning these students miss at least 10 percent of the school year, or 18 days). Tishuk says this is because many students take days off for family, cultural or religious reasons. Despite this high absentee rate, Tishuk says that students get a lot of additional school time at BSNBCS because the school year starts early (in August) and days are longfrom 8 am to 4 pm. The school offers free breakfast for students at 7:30 am and all families can take advantage of an after-school program until 6 pm. The program is free for middle school students and costs $35 a week for families of elementary school children. BSNBCSalso offers Saturday school in January, February and March to help students prepare for state tests.

The school is located in a former St John's University building, which housed a parocihal school until 2010 when BSNBCS was founded in its place. The cost of renting and maintaining these facilities takes up around 20 percent of the school's budget, but the luxuries afforded in a building with so much space are undeniable. The seven-floor structure has a gym, a large staff room on every floor, an indoor urban farm and an unused swimming pool in the basement.

In most of the classes we visited teachers directed lessons to students seated in rows or small groups. This traditional instruction is blended with opportunities for creative electives and hands-on learning. Students seemed attentive and engaged in all classrooms. BSNBCS uses the Singapore Math curriculum (in which students master fewer math concepts in greater depth) with some adjustments, and an adapted version of the Department of Education's EngageNY curriculum for English language arts. The school places a lot of emphasis on writing and offers an elective English class for middle school students. There is no science lab in the building so experiments are conducted in the classroom or in the indoor urban farm, where students learn about health and social justice as well as science. The urban farm is on the top floor of the building in a room filled with hydroponic lights; it boasts an array of fresh produce including tomatoes, kale and herbs. The farm is maintained by a "green team" of students as an after-school activity.

Elementary-grade students take art, creative writing and physical education. The school has a full-time art teacher and large dedicated art room. On our visit, a creative writing class played bingo using Dr. Seuss poems that included math. To help the transition to middle school, students in 5th grade have one teacher for math and science and another for English and history.

Middle school students can take electives like Spanish, technology and "Books to Popcorn," where students read a book then compare it to the movie adaptation.

The school has a strong project-based learning program that takes classroom lessons into the real world. For example, 5th-grade students learned about waste and created a recycling program at the school. This involved weighing garbage, taking tallies of bins around the school, creating advocacy materials and learning about the science behind recycling.

BSNBCS has made a lot of effort to ensure that families feel welcome. On the ground floor there is a family room with computers, curriculum textbooks and a conference table. On the first Friday of every month the school leadership team provides a catered breakfast for families. These breakfasts sometimes include formal presentations but usually just offer an informal opportunity for families to get face-time with school leaders and build the school's sense of community.

SPECIAL EDUCATION: BSNBCS has one special education teacher per grade. The school has some ICT team-teaching classes and students also receive push-in and/or pull-out support according to what is mandated by their individualized education program (IEP).

ADMISSIONS: By lottery with preference given to District 16, siblings and family members of current students. The school will backfill open seats at any time of year and accepts new students at all grade levels. "You can't have good schools with open seats," Tishuk said. (Ella Colley, March 2015)
