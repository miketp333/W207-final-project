The Downside

Better attendance and tone might bring this school up a notch

PS/MS 280 is a solidly average school that feels on the verge of something greater. It has the benefit of long-time leadership and a fairly stable and safe environment although there are some issues with bullying according to school surveys. Firmly established as a pre-k through 8th grade school, it is slightly over capacity and classes are large, but it also has an exciting new website and plans to further engage parents as partners in their children's learning.

A pleasant sliver of green space runs along the street near PS/MS 280, which became a full-scale elementary/middle school in 2006. The following year, the long-time principal retiredand James Weeks took the helm.The student population is mostly Hispanic, with Bengali, Arabic and Albanian speakers in the mix, as well as students from Mexico and Korea. Once a school where students consistently scored above average on state tests, it is now average in almost every way from test scores to the number of years teachers have taught to the almost perfectly balanced ratio of boys to girls.

According to the school's yearly plan, teachers are working on parent engagement. Hopefully this will boost attendance, which is respectfully, but disappointingly, average. Parents are encouraged to attend writing celebrations, awards assemblies, be classroom volunteers, chaperone trips, and participate in the interactive website which elicits their comments and contributions.

The website deserves a mention because it is one of the most engaging, student run websites we have see n. It is filled with educational tidbits, opinion polls, and pictures ranging from Picasso to the Great White Shark. It has a student E-zine with fashion news and click polls like, "What are you wearing?" (Uggs!), "What's going on?" (politics) , "What are you thinking?" (on topics like cell phone use) and "What's cooking?" [Photo at right is from the music blog]

Students benefit from a tree-lined K-2 playground, as well as a play area for upper grades, behind the school.

Special education: There are "integrated co-teaching" (ICT) classes, in which two instructorsone is a specialist in special educationteach a mix of children with special needs and general education students. There are also "self-contained" classes for children with special needs only. The school offers services including speech and occupational therapy

Admissions: Neighborhood school (Lydie Raschka, web reports and school data, August 2014)
