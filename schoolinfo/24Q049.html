Our Insights

What’s Special

A school where the arts are integrated into the curriculum.

The Downside

Overcrowding, no gym.

JANUARY 2011 UPDATE: The school, now known as PS/IS 49, serves grade K-7 and will add eighth grade in the 2011-2012 school year. With a newly constructed annex, the building has a new gymnasium and cafeteria along with classrooms.  However, overcrowding continue with 35 students in seventh grade classrooms.

NOVEMBER 2003 REVIEW: Longtime PS 49 Principal Anthony Lombardi inspires strong feelings. United Federation of Teachers President Randi Weingarten has called him a "tyrant" for weeding out teachers who didn't meet his expectations. And Lombardi concedes that in his early years, a "bloodbath" eliminated teachers who did not meet his high expectations or agree with his ideas for the school. But parents and remaining teachers admire what he's done. In fact the PS 49 we saw is a happy place, even if it lacks a gym and is located in a small, overcrowded building where every nook and cranny is occupied.

The happiness begins outside the front door, where daisies grow in warm weather. Inside, arts are central to instruction, perhaps not surprising given that Lombardi majored in poetry in college and has portraits of Frank Sinatra prominently displayed in his office. Thanks to PS 49 alumnus and businessman Tom Petrosino, every classroom has stereo equipment to listen to music. Students study song lyrics to make connections to the curriculum, so, for example, 4th graders studying the Great Depression might listen to "I've Got Plenty of Nothing." The school library has been turned into an art room, with the books on the shelves serving mostly as reference materials for teachers. Students also study slides of great art works and participate in such projects as creating Picasso-inspired Cubist shapes for math class.

Does the attention to arts hurt test scores? Apparently not. PS 49 made the state's list of 200 most-improved schools in 2002. Lombardi attributes the success to the school's strict emphasis on teaching, reflected in its decision to hire extra teachers for each grade rather than an assistant principal.

Several years ago, the school switched from a traditional Dick-and-Jane method of teaching reading to a more progressive approach developed at Columbia University's Teachers College. We saw kids sprawled on the floor, writing notes on sticky papers, chatting over books or reading quietly. One benefit of this curriculum according to teacher Mary Shannon, is that kids are "writing a lot better."

Lombardi feels an affinity for his polyglot population of kids who speak Polish, Chinese, and other languages. He spoke only Italian as a child, and says he was always in trouble for questioning rules. As a result, his goal is to "teach kids to think rather than telling them what to think."

To this day, he is not afraid to speak his mind even if it makes him unpopular: On the network television show 20/20, Lombardi criticized the city's teacher tenure policies. Indeed, PS 49 selects teachers under a "school-based option plan" that gives the school greater say in hiring.

Admissions: PS 49 is a neighborhood school in a community where many children attend private or parochial schools.(This school is featured in New York City's Best Public Elementary Schools. Pamela Wheaton, November 2003)
