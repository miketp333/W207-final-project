Our Insights

What’s Special

Some innovative arts programming and a solid honors program

The Downside

Large school may overwhelm some students

A sense of true school spirit pervades William McKinley Middle School, where vivid murals cover the hallways and enthusiastic cheerleaders perform in bright blue uniforms. Located in Dyker Heights, the large school has a robust district-wide honors program and a flourishing arts curriculum.

Historically very tight on space, the McKinley building received a new wing several years ago to ease the overcrowding. The new wing has elevators and a new cafeteria. There are also two high-school-ready science labs, three music rooms (including a fully equipped keyboard room) and two gyms to accommodate CHAMPS handball, basketball, tennis, rugby and football.

Several changes over the past few years reflect a shifting school culture. Students no longer wear uniforms and are no longer separated into academies. Instead, the school now focuses on grade-specific grouping. Several after school clubs such as Cultural Communications and I Love Me are aimed at teaching good communication skills and empathy. Teachers are almost unanimously very pleased with Principal Janice Geary who has headed the school since 2002.

The school day runs from 8:39 pm to 2:57 pm, with morning tutoring available at 8am. Students have eight English language arts and math periods per week. The ELA curriculum is thematic. There is an emphasis on reading as well as writing, and as part of their homework, students are required to read for 45 minutes every day and summarize what they have read. Students take turns being the class expert, offering help to any student who needs it.

Science lab is once a week, and the classroom teacher also serves as lab teacher. Students learn to write a lab report by 8th grade, and 8th-grade Superindentent (or "honors") students take the Living Environment Regents. A new program is being instituted in which 6th-graders will work on independent, inquiry-based, student-driven studies in addition to core classes.

The school, which participates in the Junior National Honor Society, has five Superintendent classes per grade. These students are selected based on their 3rd-grade state test scores. All honors students take Spanish language in 6th grade (general education students wait until 8th grade) and must maintain an 85 average in each subject. Tutoring is offered during lunch up to three times a week as necessary to students in the G&T program, and is also available for non G&T students. In addition, the school created an in-house honors program for students that maintain a 95 and above average, allowing them to take two Regents.

McKinley is a true haven for the arts. Entire hallways are covered floor-to-ceiling with colorful student murals in a myriad of styles. Some celebrate the city and its landmarks; others are beautiful images from cultures around the globe. The process of painting is ongoing, and continues to attract media attention. In 2012, portions of the mural dedicated to September 11 were featured on the Channel 2 news.

Here, art informs academics as well. The McKinley Fractal Math Club was formed in the fall of 2010 by math teacher and former accountant Bryan P. Stern. (Fractals are very complex shapes that are formed by repeating a simple process over and over.) Students in the club conduct math investigations, build three-dimensional models, create works of art using computer software, and connect the mathematical patterns in fractals to similar patterns in nature. In 2012, the club put on an exhibit of its artwork at nearby Gallery 364.

In addition to visual arts, the school offers drama, dance and music. On our visit, we were treated to music by the marching band and later by the gentle sounds of the string orchestra. The music teacher we encountered was excited about her work and eager to welcome new students, from beginners to advanced players.

Most students go on to Fort Hamilton High School.

Special education: McKinley is partially wheelchair accessible, and offers some self-contained classes for students with disabilities as well as Integrated Co-Teaching. The school provides counseling, speech therapy, physical therapy and occupational therapy as needed.

Admission: Zoned, except for the Superintendent's Program, for which students in District 20 will be admitted based on a combination of factors making up a "composite score". The final 4th grade report card counts for 45 percent, state tests 45 percent, attendance five percent and punctuality five percent. (Eliana Mascio, November 2013; admissions updated January 2015)
