What’s Special

The Downside

Struggles to maintain safety and respect

IS 291 has long struggled to meet the needs of students living in a neighborhood marked by poverty. In 2012 was put on a list of faiing schools by the Department of Education, often the first step of the school closing process.

Long-time principal Sean Walsh passed away in 2011 and was replaced by former assistant principal Jaqueline Rosado who gets good marks from teachers on the Learning Environment Survey. Still, academics often take a back seat to crisis and conflict management and among those who suffer most academically are English language learners and children with disabilities according to the 2012-2013 Quality Review. Safety has long been a concern and although the situation has improved according to the survey, slightly more than half the students say they do not treat each other, or adults, with respect.

On the positve side, attendance and punctuality have improved, according to the Quality Review.

Staffers make phone calls and home visits to keep on top of attendance, and suspensions fell from 58 in 2012 to 17 in 2013. Students have classes three times a week in the fitness room, equipped with bicycles, weights and treadmills [photo at right from the school's website]. The extended day period has been expanded to provide extra help for English language learners, those with disabilities and students scoring in the bottom-third, according to the Quality Review.

The school shares space with Bushwick Community High School, a transfer school that admits only older teens who are making up credits, and Math, Engineering, and Science Academy, a college-prep charter high school. The building has metal detectors and security cameras.

English as a Second Language: There are eight Spanish/English bilingual classes, and students also receive additional support with an ESL teacher inside and outside their classrooms.

Special education:There are Integrated Co-Teaching (ICT) classes, where two faculty members, one of whom is certified in special education, teach a class that mixes both general and special education students and there are classes for only children with special needs.

After school: Students receive academic tutoring until 5 pm Tuesday through Friday. Activities include basketball, soccer, art, chess, drama and chorus. Students may also participate in a Saturday Academy from 9 to 11 am.

Admissions: Zoned neighborhood school (Lydie Raschka, updated from web reports and DOE statistics, September, 2013)
