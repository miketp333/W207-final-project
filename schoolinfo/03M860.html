Our Insights

What’s Special

A robotics program; growing relationship with Columbia University

The Downside

Test scores have a ways to go

Frederick Douglass Academy II offers small classes, uniforms and a mix of lecture-style lessons and creative projects and discussion. There are two tracks in the high school: the Science, Math, Engineering and Technology (STEM) Institute, and the Humanities Institute, which integrates language and the arts. The curriculum is designed with the help of Teacher's College, Columbia University, located just a few blocks away.

Once considered a strong school, serving students with solid reading, writing and math abilities, FDA II faltered after moving to its current location in 2005, unprepared for the overwhelming needs of a different population. As charter schools opened in the area, the numbers of students with special needs rose at FDA II according to Principal Osei Owusu-Afriyie, who took the helm in 2010. In 2011 the middle school was at risk of closing with test scores among the worst in the city, a very high suspension rate and declining enrollment.

But the Department of Education decided to keep both the middle school and the high school open, citing the progress it had made under Owusu-Afriyie. "My mission is returning the school back to what it was, reconnecting it to its college prep roots," said Afriyie, a former principal of Eagle Academy for Young Men.

Classes are held on the third and fourth floors of a building shared with Wadleigh Secondary School and Success Academy Harlem West. Although all three schools would like more room, they seem to have worked out space sharing issues. Wadleigh and FDA II students have a joint prom and take some college trips together.

Upon his arrival, Afriyie spruced up the corridors with fresh paint, addressed a rodent problem and decorated the hallways with cheery banners. He met with every teacher and arranged the schedule so they could plan lessons together. In addition to art, band and chorus, he started advanced placement classes in English Literature and Composition, Calculus and U.S. History.

Perhaps most importantly, Afriyie harnessed the expertise of his best teachers, who serve as instructional leaders assisting less experienced staff, some of whom are former student teachers hired directly after graduation from Hunter College. An English teacher started an independent reading program, with the goal that every student will read 25 books per year and time has been built into the schedule to make it happen. Another teacher started a robotics program.

Attendance and test scores are improving. Staffers credit this not only to a calmer atmosphere with more accountability and streamlined lesson formats, but also to Town Hall gatherings, a time for announcements and celebrations of achievement. While these improvements indicate progress and the atmosphere is safer according to the 2012 Learning Environment Survey, 57 percent of students said students do not always treat teachers with respect. Afriyie said the advisory program and electives based on teacher interests such as graphic novels and debate are attempts to build more positive relationships. An instructor from Columbia University teaches a popular business entrepreneurship elective.

We saw caring teachers leading creative lessons. Ninth graders were cutting out pictures of gods and goddesses in order to make a "cosmogram," or visual diagram, in preparation for a unit on the Odyssey. Popular math teacher Marietta Geraldino has sparked a love of math for many students with her funny, warm delivery: "I can sense some kind of negativity here," she said, hovering over a group of four struggling students, "keep stayin' alive!"

However, there is still much work to do to turn out college-ready graduates. Test scores remain below average in most subjects. And a senior said she has rarely written a paper that is more than two pages long.

Special education: The graduation rate for children with Individual Educational Plans was 83% in 2012. Students receive tutoring in small groups twice a week, get special help in class and have their own guidance counselor who offers emotional help around self-esteem and social issues.

College: Forty-seven out of 53 graduates enrolled in college in 2012 (some chose the military). A full-time college counselor assists students with applications. Top students have been accepted to New York University, Brown, Wheaton and Michigan State.

Admissions: The middle school screening process involves an interview. The administration said they are looking for families committed to their mission and with good attendance. The middle school is open to students in District 3; current 8th-graders and those from districts 3 and 5 get priority in high school admissions. (Lydie Raschka, April 2013).

Programs and Admissions

The Douglass STEM Institute
Admissions Method: Ed. Opt.
Program Description

The Douglass STEM Institute prepares scholars for success in colleges and careers focused on science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM) through a performance-task-based curriculum. In addition, to our core Science and Math sequence, scholars have access to Advanced Placement courses as well as electives focused on robotics and engineering. Scholars will be equipped with a foundation of essential knowledge and skills to critically explore and solve real world problems.
