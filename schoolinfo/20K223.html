Our Insights

What’s Special

Energetic staff and small class size

Since Gertrude Adduci came aboard as principal in January 2003, IS 223 has begun a transformation. New assistant principals and teachers have joined the staff; old, filthy windows have been replaced; and the school feels newly energized. Administrators hope all this will help IS 223 shed its designation as a "corrective action" school -- one whose students have not made adequate progress on state tests for at least two years.

Students are assigned to one of three "academies" -- Artists and Writers, Math and Science, and International -- overseen primarily by an assistant principal. Because each academy occupies a separate floor in the building, hallways don't become too crowded during class changes.

During our visit, class sizes in all three academies were small and populated with lively kids. Desks were usually arranged in groups, and teachers were approachable. We saw a lot of old and worn books, although they were being slowly replaced by the books needed in the new, citywide curriculum.

Some students seemed distracted, talking constantly or, on occasion, disrupting the class. But once they settled down, they were able to do the work demanded of them. Indeed, many students were attentive and eager to ask questions or volunteer answers, whether working on algebraic equations or discussing the formation of sedimentary rocks.

The International Academy houses the school's large population of English Language Learners (ELL), recent immigrants not yet proficient in English. Bilingual classes are offered and students receive at least 12 periods of ESL instruction a week. The curriculum, as in the rest of the school, is a balance of lectures, group work and test prep. We sensed that the staff was motivated by a real sense of goodwill toward and understanding of their students, and the kids seemed to respond, feeling comfortable speaking in class. The ESL coordinator encourages his eighth graders to participate in CUNY's free, six-week intensive English language program, which helps prepare ELLs for high school.

Because of its "corrective action" status, IS 223 is on the list of schools from which, under the federal No Child Left Behind law, students may transfer. This has brought an unexpected boon -- a decrease in enrollment that has enabled IS 223 to maintain small classes. But the staff is optimistic that IS 223 will be taken off the failing school list, and to accommodate a return to higher enrollment numbers, the administration plans to divide large classrooms to create more, and smaller, classes.

 There is a free YMCA after school program in the school.

Admissions: Located in a largely Hasidic Jewish area where many children attend private religious schools, IS 223 has many spots for children outside the immediate neighborhood. (Catherine Man, October 2003)
