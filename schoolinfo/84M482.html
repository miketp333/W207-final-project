What’s Special

The Downside

Strict discipline

Harlem Success Academy 5 is the fifth Harlem franchise of the popular Success Academy network of charter schools led by Eva Moskowitz, a former chair of the City Council's education committee. The familiar Success Academy traits are all present here, from the plaid jumpers and orange ties on students to the numbers and words painted on the hallway floors (lest kids lined up between classes miss a learning opportunity).

Harlem Success Academy 5 opened in August 2010 on the third floor of a large, modern building shared rather contentiously with PS 123, a combined elementary and middle school. The new Success Academy arrived just after the fast-growing Harlem Success Academy 2 moved to a new site in East Harlem. Harlem Success Academy 5 opened with kindergarten and 1st grade in its inaugural year and added a grade annually until it reached K-4.

The classes we saw during a one-hour visit in April 2011 were alluring advertisements for the Success Academy model. The bright and inviting carpeted classrooms were equipped with sophisticated SMARTboards, and hallways were decorated with student artwork and inspirational posters, as well as reminders of Success Academy rules. Children seemed polite and attentive, and many seemed to be performing above grade level. A class of kindergartners easily counted aloud to 100 by 2s. One 5-year-old girl proudly read a book about cookies.

Classroom instruction includes songs or student-to-student reinforcement. When several kids moved up one level in their reading skills, their class honored them with the song "I Like to Move It, Move It." Students often study in small groups based on ability, and occasionally struggling 1st graders might learn alongside kindergartners, or bright kindergartners will be allowed to join a 1st-grade lesson.

Founding principal Stacey Apatov began her career through Teach for America as a 1st-grade teacher at PS 138 in the Bronx. After two years, Apatov left in 2006 to teach at newly opened Harlem Success Academy 1 and later was placed in charge of developing the charter networks science curriculum. This is her first time as a principal. [In 2013, Apatov became director of science and math for the Success Academy network. She was replaced by Khari Shabazz, former dean of students. Molly Cohen became principal in2016.]

Harlem Success Academy 5's academic year begins in August with long school days, from 7:45 am until 3:45 pm. Students take music and dance every other day and chess classes once a week. Rules designed to keep order (such as No talking in the hallways) are strictly enforced, and youngsters who misbehave get timeouts lasting up to five minutes.

Students in Harlem Success Academy 5 and PS 123 have little interaction with each other and take turns using the cafeteria, which means Success Academy kids eat lunch at 1 pm.

Sharing the buildings gym has proved difficult, so Success Academy kids get outdoor recess in the schools large exercise yard in all kinds of weather, even during winter. "We have snowball fights. The kids build snowmen," Apatov said. "We try to send them out no matter what."

The school's open-door policy lets parents drop in and observe their children's studies for up to 30 minutes. Parents also are asked to read high-quality books to students every night, and kids are expected to take home and read at least 22 books a month.

Harlem Success Academy's teachers are non-union, but Apatov said she has no shortage of applicants for open positions. "The applicants we get are 'I want to change the world' types. They are so committed and so dedicated." Staff members regularly get feedback and professional-development courses to help them quickly improve.

Special education: Extra help is available is the SETSS (Special Education Teacher Support Services) room. Speech therapy and occupational therapy are also available. By fall 2011, Apatov hopes to have at least one ICT classroom in place.

Admissions: Admission is by lottery. Preference is given to siblings, English-language learners, students in District 5, and students characterized as at risk because they are zoned for a failing school. (Skip Card, April 2011; principal and time update 2016)
