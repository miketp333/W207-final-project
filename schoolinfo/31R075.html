Our Insights

What’s Special

Honors program lets 8th-graders take three Regents, strong special education programs

The Downside

Students remain in noisy lunchroom for full period, awkward building

IS 75 is very much a neighborhood school. Most students come from the surrounding South Shore Staten Island neighborhoods and remain nearby for high school. Teacher turnover is low, attendance fairly high. Despite the school's large size, people are friendly; a security guard readily volunteered that its a "great school," and principal Kenneth Zapata seems to know the students, bantering with many of the boys about local sports teams.

While less racially diverse than many city middle schools, IS 75 serves students with a wide range of academic interests and abilities. Those in the Scholars program take an accelerated program with three Regents examsintegrated algebra, living environment and U.S. historyin 8th grade. The school also has a high percentage of students with special needs and houses a District 75 program as well as ASD NEST for students on the autism spectrum. While IS 75 students do well on standardized tests, the school does not have the high-pressure academic environment found in many academically rigorous middle schools.

Zapata, a former assistant principal at Susan E. Wagner High School and an English teacher, said the school is keeping its Teachers College English program and hasnot had to make substantial changes to adjust to the Common Core. It has stepped up efforts to introduce more "authentic texts"sources other than textbooksin social studies and science and to find materials that challenges readers at all levels.

For math, IS 75 is cautiously using a Pearson program to meet Common Core requirements, but Zapata hopes to adapt it to offer more teacher-led instruction. Much of the effort in math goes toward breaking down word problems and teaching vocabulary. "A lot of the time it isnt that the kids dont know math, it's that they dont know what question is being asked," Zapata said. A math coach has created the school's own math assessments with instant evaluations, enabling teachers to adjust their classwork and assignments accordingly.

Everyone at IS 75 has eight periods of math a week. Students who need extra help may take an additional period of English or math instead of a minor subject. Eighth-graders with difficulties in reading or math are assigned to small classesabout 15 students eachfor those subjects.

Classes are mostly well-taught, although some of the work on display did not seem particularly difficult. On the day of our visit, students generally paid attention and participated. Eighth-graders in an English class seemed eager to start writing guidebooks on a topic of their own choosing. They also had been reading "West Side Story" and will each design and write their own Playbills for a real or imaginary production of the play.

In a science class, the teacher led an animated discussion about how to reduce friction, starting with the example of how a student might avoid rug burn sliding down a carpeted stair. "Don't try this at home," she cautioned.

Students in the Scholars program can study either Spanish or Italian for three years. Other students take a language for two years. Children can focus on one arts areaband, chorus, drama or visual artfor all three years. Others take an array of minor subjects such as robotics, computer design and art.

After 8th grade, almost all the children remain on Staten Island, with many going to the local high school,Tottenville,and some attending Wagner or private schools. Many are admitted to Staten Island Tech, a specialized high school.

The architects of the IS 75 building made odd design choices that resulted in some hallways with no classrooms and an irregularly shaped and noisy lunchroom, where students spend the entire period.

Special education: IS 75 was the first middle school on Staten Island to offer an ASD NEST program for high-functioning students on the autism spectrum. The school has other team-teaching classes, as well as self-contained classes and is pioneering a program where children in a self-contained class spend the day in a large well-equipped room with one teacher and learn life skills as well as academic subjects.

Admissions: The school accepts all students in its zone but does have about 30 slots in the Scholars program for students from elsewhere on Staten Island. Admission to this programis based on a combination of factors that make up a "composite score"the final 4th grade report card counts for 45 percent, state tests 45 percent, punctuality five percent and attendance five percent.(Gail Robinson, May 2014; admissions updated January 2015)
