Our Insights

What’s Special

Over 20 enrichment programs in the arts and physical education.

The Downside

School undergoing transition with new leadership

Future Leaders Institute has a long school day that includes activities such as African drumming, African dance and track and field. Class size ranges from 16 in kindergarten to 23 in upper grades. The school strives to prepare children for selective high schools, and about 40 percent of graduates go on to Roman Catholic schools.

 

Every class has science daily. Even the oldest students get recess for 20 minutes, a structured time with “stations” like basketball, cheerleading and jungle gym. On our visit we saw 7th graders reading poems by William Blake and Emily Dickenson. In another classroom 10 middle schoolers sat in a circle on chairs for peer mediation training.

 

The atmosphere is relaxed if a bit unsettled, perhaps a reflection of the fact that more than  80 percent of the teaching staff had been at FLI three years or less at the time of our visit. While moving from class to class 8th-graders jostled each other good-naturedly in the narrow hallway built for younger children. Teacher voices were mostly patient and kind. Boisterous students in a middle school classroom could be overheard easily from the small, sparsely furnished main office. Over the din a teacher said, “Enough!” Coats spilled out of closets onto the floor in a couple of rooms and there was at least one child in every room sitting off to the side apparently for behavior issues.

 

The school underwent a difficult transition when its founders, Marc Waxman and Gianna Cassetta, left in 2008. Principal Ismael “Izzy” Colon and Stephanie Carroll, director of curriculum and instruction, were hired in 2010 to shore up academics and school culture and to help stem rising turnover among staff. Initiatives include mentors for every teacher, targeted support for reading and math, and more staff collaboration. “There is more continuity between what, when and how,” said 1st grade teacher Kate Bannon.

 

Future Leaders is housed on two floors of a long building shared with Young Diplomat’s Academy. Behind the school is a cheery, spacious playground with climbing equipment and a track. The school day runs from 8:30 a.m. to 5 p.m. All students except kindergartners participate in enrichment classes from 3 pm to 5 pm Monday through Thursday. 

 

 

 

Students in grades 5-8 are offered sessions to prepare them for admissions exams to private schools, the ISEE or SSAT, during the summer and during lunch and recess. Extra academic support is offered to all students daily after school and twice weekly in the mornings. Reading teachers push into K-5 classrooms each day to provide an hour of extra help as needed.

 

Special Education: Five special education teachers work in classrooms with individuals as needed. One class has a combination of special and general education students with two teachers, one trained in special education. Fourteen percent of students get services but this will likely increase. “We will modify our program to fit any child who comes, with compassion, care and support,” said Teri Berman, director of special education.

 

Admissions: Lottery with preference to District 3. Parents should be aware that the school day goes until 5 pm. Summer remedial classes are held in the month of July with the first day of the new school year beginning of August. (Lydie Raschka, March 2011)
