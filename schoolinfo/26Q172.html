The Downside

Some students do not treat each other with respect

MS 172 is located in a three-story building with well-equipped classrooms that overlook trees and athletic courts. Despite a gentle decline in enrollment in recent years and an increase in numbers of students with special needs, the school continues to enjoy solid test scores and to offer honors classes, video production and an active after school program.

Classes are tracked into three groups: extra help, regular and SP (Special Progress), an honors track for 7th and 8th graders. SP students take Regents-level algebra and earth science classes in 8th grade. The upper grades move from subject to subject but this is reduced in 6th grade to make the transition from elementary to middle school easier. Sixth graders are eligible for an honors track called the Scholars Program.Beyond the usual core academics, MS 172 offers instrumental music, chorus, drama, computer and art. Parents are invited to workshops on how to use the internet, test prep tactics and more.

The school day begins with an old-fashioned reciting of the Pledge of Allegiance, but some kids grumble that students do not treat each other with respect. One initiative to bolster better relations is the South Asian Youth Action (SAYA), a girls empowerment group that meets twice weekly to help girls explore topics related to self-confidence, respect and peer pressure. [picture of SAYA girls posted here] Rising Stars awards go to students who display citizenship, creativity, responsibility, perseverance, leadership and other positive qualities and there is also a peer mediation program.

Principal Jeffery Slivko took the helm in 2004 after serving as assistant principal at MS 74 .

MS 172 graduates attend Queens High School of Teaching, Bayside, Francis Lewis and Martin Van Buren High School, among others.

Admissions: Zoned neighborhood school. Admission to the gifted and talented programis based on a combination of factors that make up a "composite score"the final 4th grade report card counts for 35 percent, state tests 35 percent, student performance indicators 20 percent and attendance ten percent.(Lydie Raschka, updated with web reports and DOE data, October 2013; admissions updated January 2015)
