Our Insights

What’s Special

Strong arts program; a lot of guidance and counseling for needy students

The Downside

Many classrooms are windowless

IS 25 offers solid academics, a good variety of music instruction and plenty of social and emotional guidance for the turbulent adolescent years. Once a very traditional place with strict discipline, IS 25 has embraced a more holistic approach to education over the years.

Academically, IS 25 has something for everyone. In addition to general education classes, there are a variety of choices for children with special needs including team-taught classes that mix general and special education students together in one room.The school has three SP (Special Placement) or honors classes in each grade and top students may participate in Regents-level Algebra and Living Environment classes in the 8th grade.

Staff members "adopt" students in need of extra support, checking up on them in classrooms and after school. The school has a drug and alcohol prevention program housed in a cozy, wallpapered office with a rocking chair and cushioned furniture strewn with pillows.During their three years at IS 25, students get instruction in topics such as bullying, anger management and peer counseling. The Child Center of New York offers counseling for students and their families at the school.

Principal Mary Ellen Kociszewski Beirne replaced principal Joseph Catone in 2009, when he retired. She was previously the principal at the High School for Applied Communication and taught for many years in parochial schools.

Teachers invest a lot of energy and resources into their talent classes: vocal music, band, dance, keyboard and chorus (the art talent was cut due to lack of money).Students are screened in elementary school for placement in one of the talent programs and stay in their chosen track for all three years.

IS 25 is located in the District 25 neighborhood of Flushing, steps from the border with District 26. It's housed in a large building erected in the 1970s, a period that favored the open school model. Many classrooms are windowless and divided by retractable walls. World Journalism Preparatory: A College Board School and a few classes from a District 75 program occupy space in the building and share access to the building's library, gym, auditorium and cafeteria.

Eighth graders participate in Urban Advantage, an organization sponsoring science programs at city venues including the Museum of Natural History, Bronx Zoo, and New York Botanical Gardens. Teaching artists from Carnegie Hall, the Julliard School of Music, Weill Music Institute, and Queens College Jazz Project work with talent classes.

The Child Center of New York sponsors after-school activities including homework help, tutoring and sports that run until 6 p.m.Sports teams in fitness, volleyball, football, softball, tennis, and basketball participate in the C.H.A.M.P.S. league. There is a drama club and a track club. Spanish is the only foreign language taught.

English Language Learners: A full-time English as a Second Language (ESL) instructor provides support for students still learning English.


Popular high school choices are Townsend Harris, Bayside, Cardozo, Francis Lewis as well as the citys specialized high schools for those who meet the requirements, as many usually do.

Admissions: Neighborhood school. Placement in 6th grade SP classes is based upon a student's 5th grade report card and test scores(Laura Zingmond, February 2009, updated with web reports and DOE data, Lydie Raschka, October 2013).
