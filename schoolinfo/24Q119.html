Our Insights

What’s Special

Goal-based initiatives move students and teachers toward progress

The Downside

Special education classes concentrated in handicap-accessible wing

IS 119 provides students with an outlet for their artistic talents along with strong academic classes in which they are pushed to do their very best. In 2011 this middle school expanded to include an elementary school and will add one grade per year until it has reached its full capacity as a K-8 school in 2016. It has a gifted program and honors classes.

Glendale is a closely-knit community that remains much as it has for years, with attractive one-or two-family homes inhabited by descendants of Italian and German settlers teachers, blue-collar workers and city employees. People who grow up in Glendale tend to stay, so it's not surprising that many parents send their children to the same middle school they attended.

In 2008 the school welcomed a new principal, Dr. Jeanne Fagan, who introduced initiatives to improve educational standards for students at all academic levels. Under her guidance the citys report card for the school has rapidly improved despite increasing numbers of children with special needs.

Formerly assistant principal at nearby IS 77 in Ridgewood, Fagan has sought ways to encourage already high-achieving students to push their scores even higher from Level 3s to Level 4s on state exams. To do so, she introduced a new ELA curriculum and a schoolwide initiative, where both teachers and students must track their individual goals for each marking period. She also brought order to a school with a history of sometimes unruly behavior.

In language classes students focus on non-fiction, non-narrative literature to better prepare for college. In math classes, students take pre-tests when they start a new unit of study, and then wrap up the unit with a post-test to show their growth, or trouble-shoot problem areas. There are double periods in key subject areas where individual and small group instruction is the norm, according to the quality review.

A strong performing arts program includes chorus, dance, drama, and instrumental music (boasting a concert band, jazz band, and string orchestra). There are concerts in both the winter and the spring, and an annual school musical.

Teachers work with struggling students in the extended day program in small groups and focus on improving students writing skills. Teachers try out new strategies, and then meet with their teacher team to discuss which strategies work well and can be used in the classroom with all students.

Students study either Italian or Spanish in the 7th and 8th grades, taking the Regents proficiency exam before graduation. Top students may take Regents-level Algebra and Earth Science courses in 8th grade.

The four-story, traditional 1930s building in which the school is housed has two gymnasiums and an auditorium, as well as a modern annex where most 6th-grade classes are located. Special education classes tend to be clustered in the newer wing, as well, because it is handicap-accessible, unlike the older building.

Special education:IS 119 has a growing special education population. The school offers SETSS, self-contained classes, and team-taught classes that mix children with special needs with general education students.

English language learners: Two English as Second Language teachers assist the roughly 60 English language learners who speak Spanish, Polish, Albanian, Arabic, Chinese and a smattering of other languages.

Gifted and talented: IS 119 is one of two host schools for District 24s Beacon program for gifted and talented students. (IS 61 in Corona is the other host school.) Additionally, IS 119 has one honors class on both the 7th and 8th grades for students who are not in the Beacon program. Entry into the honors class is based on scores on state ELA and math exams, students grades, an in-house exam and teacher recommendations. Students in both the Beacon and honors programs take the algebra and earth science Regents exams. Students will also take the US History and Government Regents, an exam typically taken by high school juniors. The exam will be the culmination of two years of study, though in most high schools, US history is generally taught in one year.

After school: IS 119 works with Odyssey of the Mind, a program that provides creative, problem-solving projects. The Math Team and Drama Club also attract a large number of students.

After graduation: Graduates tend to move on to small high schools throughout Queens and there is preparation for the specialized high school exam.

Admissions: Zoned neighborhood school. (Dan Fletcher and Cristin Strining, October 2009, updated from web reports and dOE data, October 2013)
