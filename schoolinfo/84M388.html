St. HOPE Leadership Academy Charter School

Our Insights

What’s Special

Students get personal attention from teachers; flexibility in curriculum and staffing

The Downside

Limited electives; no foreign language is taught. Some students chafe at the strict discipline

December 2014 update: In 2012, founding Principal Ventura Rodriguez left St. HOPE and moved to Massachusetts. The school's current principal is Dr. Constance Bond. MS 344 closed in 2012. St. HOPE, PS 92 and Democracy Prep Charter School are still co-located. 

January 2011 review: Few middle schoolers enjoy strict rules, heavy workloads and nosy teachers, so it’s no surprise to hear students at St. HOPE Leadership Academy grumble a bit. Yet parents unhappy with traditional public schools in this Harlem neighborhood eagerly enter the lottery for a seat in this charter school, hoping that it will steer their children toward high school and college. St. HOPE emphasizes personal attention and rigorous academics within a shell of tough love and rigid discipline.

“We want to educate the kids that need the support the most,” said Ventura Rodriguez, the founding principal. Originally envisioned as a K-12 school, St. HOPE (modeled after a school of the same name in Sacramento, California) began as a middle school to address what the city saw as the greatest need. Student test scores have not been exactly stellar since it opened in 2008, but Rodriguez said the school’s charter forces him to quickly address such problems. “We have a lot greater freedom and flexibility in exchange for accountability,” he said. “We could be closed down if we don’t achieve.”

HOPE is an acronym for Helping Others Pursue Excellence, but the schools core values have evolved to spell HARLEM: Honor, Absolute determination, Responsibility, Leadership, Excellence and Mission. The school takes up most of two floors in an open, light-filled building amicably shared with an elementary school, PS 92 (the Mary McLeod Bethune School) and another charter school, Democracy Prep Charter High School School. St. HOPE uses facilities at a nearby YMCA rather than share the building’s gym.

Though the school’s small size means teachers get to know students personally, it also limits the number of electives, and the school offers no foreign language courses. “It’s hard to have classes that are much more specialized when you have such a limited population,” Rodriguez said. Curriculums are structured to move kids up the ladder both academically and socially. In 5th grade, they learn the “essential 55,” a laundry list of good habits. By 8th grade, the focus is on selecting and applying to a high school. Students also visit two colleges each year, in part to demystify the college experience.

Students must wear identical uniforms (blue polo shirts bearing the school logo and tan pants), talking is not allowed in hallways during class changes, and students disrupting class discussion are quickly shushed. The same rule applies to teachers, and Rodriguez has used his charter school’s flexibility to fire teachers who didn’t meet expectations.

Eighth-graders we spoke to described the school as a big family, with both the good and bad that implies. Teachers paid attention (sometimes too much) and kept them focused on the course work, they said. Few liked St. HOPE’s uniforms and rules, but most had a grudging admiration for an institution they realized was pushing them in a good direction. “St. HOPE has changed me,” said Raihanata, an 8th grader. “I’m more mature. I know how to handle my problems.”

The school also emphasizes the idea of giving back and requires students to perform community service—from cleaning up parks to writing letters to soldiers overseas.

Special education: Approximately one-fifth of students have special needs. “Those are the kids that are coming to us, so we had to respond to that need,” Rodriguez said. Students with IEPs are often taught in ICT classes featuring two teachers and two specialists. Special-education support staff is available for tutoring and other assistance.

Admissions: St. HOPE annually accepts 75 students for its 5th-grade class. Selection is by lottery, with preference given to siblings and families living in District 5. Lottery is held in April. In 2010, more than 600 students applied. (Skip Card, January 2011; updated by Ella Colley, December 2014)
