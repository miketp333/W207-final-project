Our Insights

What’s Special

A sweet, orderly neighborhood school

The Downside

Parent involvement is lacking

A sweet, orderly school with a collegial staff and a dynamic principal, Sunset Park Prep gives students both the academic skills and the hand-holding they need to get along in high school and the wider world. Many students are children of immigrants from Latin America and, increasingly, from China and the Middle East. The school enrolls many siblings, and even children of staff members.

Housed on the 3rd and 4th floors of a brick building constructed in 1901, Sunset Park shares space with MS 136. The building is old but spotless, with tall windows that let in lots of light, high ceilings and oak coat closets. Some of the rooms are painted in soft pastels. There is a wide-open view of the surrounding neighborhood, ocean liners in the river and Manhattan's skyscrapers.

Class changes are smooth, with kids moving quietly through the corridors and settling quickly into their work. The classrooms have bins of fun-to-read fiction books, alongside many non-fiction books in keeping with new Common Core standards.
.
"We know our areas of weakness," said Jennifer Spalding who started at the school in 2005 with Teach for America and became principal in 2013. "Most students are former ELLs [English Language Learners] and with the Common Core and more writing, it's a challenge."

To help prepare them, students are given more writing instruction, including checklists and sets of rubrics for each grade. Children write is in all subjects, not just English Language Arts (ELA), and student essays are posted throughout the corridors. Teachers make time for reading every day. The Teachers College reading and writing workshop curriculum is supplemented by lessons in grammar and vocabulary, including a "word of the day."

There are eight periods of math and ELA per week, some of which are 90 minutes long. Inventive math teachers give multi-step problems and provide interesting dilemmas for students to solve. One teacher "wrecked" the classroom before students came in, moving desks, tables and paraphernalia. Then he asked the students to look for objects that were similar and to put the room back together. This helped them learn how to sort and organize, which came in handy in a study of fractions and decimals, said Spalding. "It's not just drill and kill," she said. All 8th-graders take algebra and some take the Regents exam. Some students also take and pass the Living Environment Regents, for high school credit.

Sunset Park Prep places a premium on character education. The principal is a fan of Paul Tough's book, How Children Succeed."Students need grit and students need perseverance. It's a really important message," she said.

Working with Morningside Center for Social Responsibility, students may become mediators after three days of training and passing a test. The mediation cuts across all grades; 6th-graders may mediate 8th-grader disputes. There are also group anti-bullying initiatives, including assemblies. "It's not difficult for 25 kids to stand up for one kid," the principal said.

An "O" for "outstanding" sheet moves with the students from class to class. A "O" is earned when the teacher finds, "Oh, this class was outstanding." Winning classrooms may go bowling or get another reward.

Sixth grade students take classes in visual arts, 7th-graders in drama and 8th-graders, music. Students also get three periods each week for enrichment activities, which might be an extra lab in science or a book or music club. The ambitious drama program helps kids put on two productions each year, of musicals such as a Broadway revue and "Alice in Wonderland."

Each grade goes on an out-of-town trip: 6th-graders go to the Poconos, 7th-graders to Philadelphia or Boston and 8th-graders to Washington, DC.

Sunset Park Prep graduates tend to stay in the neighborhood, attending high schools such as Telecommunications, Fort Hamilton and New Utrecht; a few go further afield to specialized schools including Stuyvesant (two graduates in 2013) and Brooklyn Tech, or the Museum School in Manhattan.Bard High School comes to the school to give kids their entrance test. "Parents don't want them to go too far," the parent coordinator said.

Parent involvement isn't what it is at some other District 15 schools, so the guidance counselor works closely with 8th-graders on their high school applications. Many parents are not computer-literate and don't have e-mail, said the parent coordinator, so she has begun to offer computer classes for them.

Homework and grades are posted online for parents and students to see.

Special education: There are two Integrated Co-Teaching classes on each grade. They have two teachers, one of whom is certified in special education, and a mix of general education and special needs children. Sunset Park Prep also offers one separate special education class that serves only special needs children.

English Language Learners: Many teachers speak Spanish and one speaks Mandarin. Languages spoken include Arablic, Mandarin and Spanish.

After school: Lifeline offers an after school program from 4 pm to 6 pm, also used by MS 136. The school is open from 6 pm to 9 pm for the Beacon program. Some clubs and activities, such as basketball, LEGO robotics, drama and choir, meet from 7 am to 8 am. There's also a Russian club. The school uses tennis courts and a field behind the school for baseball, soccer and other sports.

Admissions: District 15. The school looks for students who have an average of 2.5 on state standardized tests, fewer than 10 absences and good grades.(Pamela Wheaton, November 2013)
