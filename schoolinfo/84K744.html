NOVEMBER 2014 UPDATE: As of fall 2011, Coney Island Preparatory Public Charter School shares space with IS 303 and Rachel Carson High School for Coastal Studies. There-siting was meant to bea temporary solution until the end of the 2013-14 school year, however the school was still located in the building as of fall 2014.

JANUARY 2011 REVIEW: With a college-prep focus even in 5th grade and a motto of "safe and structured," Coney Island Prep Charter sets expectations high. Classes begin at 7:30 a.m. and end at 4 p.m., except on Wednesdays when school ends at 2 p.m. The school opened in 2009 in temporary quarters in the Carey Gardens public housing community center with 81 5th graders. It plans to add a grade each year until it has grades 5 to 12.

Students move through the halls in silent, single-file lines. Students stay in their classroom for most of the day. They eat both breakfast and lunch in their classroom, and teachers-not studentschange classes for different subjects. Although there are 30 students in each class, all were engaged in the classrooms we visited, raising hands and participating in a respectful, organized manner. "We really believe that for learning to take place there must be a safe, calm environment. We put a high premium on that," said Principal Jacob Mnookin, an alumnus of Teach for America, the Woodrow Wilson School of Public Policy at Princeton, and the Fellowship for Building Excellent Schools.

Each day, students take two periods of math, one period each of reading, writing and social studies, and either gym, science or ethics. Teachers decide the curriculum during a three-week staff orientation in the summer.

Parent involvement is high, with 89 percent coming to teacher conferences in 2010, said Mnookin. Coney Island Prep holds four parent workshops a year on topics such as helping students improve their reading at home and cyber-bullying and about 50 percent of parents attend.

Students wear a uniform of a light-blue dress shirt, tie (for boys) and black or khaki dress pants.

An afterschool art club meets on Tuesdays but no sports. Students who do not need extra tutoring may participate in chess, art or basketball during the second half of the 70 minute lunch period.

Special education: The school has one Collaborative Team Teaching class (two teachers in the classroom together and one is certified in special education) for each grade.

Admissions: Students are admitted by lottery. In 2010/2011, 282 students applied for 81 5th grade spots. (Aryn Bloodworth, January 2011)
