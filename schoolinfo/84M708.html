What’s Special

The Downside

JUNE 2011 UPDATE: Harlem Day Charter School will officially be taken over by Democracy Prep Public Schools in August, 2011. Because of low test scores and failing performance, the State University of New York decided to close the school. But it also asked for bids from other charter schools to take it over. Democracy Prep stepped forward and the school, with some of the same teachers and students, will reopen as Harlem Prep Charter School.

SEPTEMBER 2008 UPDATE: Principal Jada Best left after just two years. The new principal, Patrice Ricciotti, had been an assistant principal at Harlem Day.

OCTOBER 2007 REVIEW: Founded in September 2001, Harlem Day Charter School is a small school located within Taino Towers, a high-rise apartment building with an auditorium, gym, and outdoor play space. With only two classes on each grade and a maximum of 22 students in each class, as well as assistant teachers in K-2nd grade classes, children receive more attention than they would in an ordinary neighborhood school.

Adults keep a tight rein on children's behavior. Students are required to wear neat uniforms and staffers seem to enforce the policy. Students travel with their teachers in quiet and orderly lines. Some teachers keep timers in their classrooms to make sure students don't dilly-dally. In a very quiet lunchroom, the only voice we heard was that of an adult who was scolding children for misbehavior. Principal Jada Best, who arrived in 2006 after serving as the founding principal of the Achievement First East New York Charter School in Brooklyn, said establishing a strong school-wide discipline policy was one of her first goals to ensure that "students are staying on task."

On our visit, we found most students to be engaged in class. Some teachers seemed strict but earned the respect of their students. In one class, music played as students created calendars based on the ones the Aztecs had used. We talked to outspoken and friendly 3rd graders, who could be silenced with one look from their teacher, even as they told us that they had fun with her.

The school uses Core Knowledge, a curriculum developed by educator E.D. Hirsch with the idea that there are key ideas and information that any well-educated American should know. Each room has a classroom library, so children can select their own books to read. Children who are struggling with reading may use computer software that teaches through games and stories. A reading specialist works with the neediest students. Children receive special education services mostly in the form of extra instructional support in general education classrooms with other students.

Most teachers are fairly young, and several told us they would benefit from more support. Best told us that her focus has been on discipline during her first year at the school but she intends to pay more attention to the quality of instruction.

The extended day program, which lasts from 3:15 to 5:30 p.m., offers homework help and a range of art classes (music, visual arts, even yoga) taught by artists from community arts organizations. "Here there is a clear appreciation for extended day as a part of the regular school day and the Harlem Day mission," the program coordinator told us, adding that arts instruction is not an afterthought as it is in some other schools.

To prepare them for middle school, 5th graders switch classes for different subjects. Founded as an alternative to the neighborhood's failing public schools, Harlem Day Charter School administrators encourage students to apply to private schools or other "independent" schools. Many graduates go to a relatively new charter school, Democracy Prep. (Catherine Man, October 2007)
