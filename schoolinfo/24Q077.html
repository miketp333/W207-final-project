Our Insights

What’s Special

Sports, band and other big school perks

The Downside

Test scores have a ways to go; kids say students don't treat each other with respect

A large school offering band, and a range of sports and clubs, IS 77 serves an increasing number of students with special needs and teens who speak Spanish, Arabic, Chinese and a smattering of other languages such as Russian and Polish.

Over the course of a decade, enrollment has slowly declined at this middle school and test scores have suffered. Many incoming 6th graders arrive with low skills and scores on state exams according to the Comprehensive Educational Plan. To bring up skills, before and after school programs are offered in specific academic subject areas. Teachers use a highly structured reading program, as well as videos and songs to teach new words in a fun way. Top students may join honors classes.

Children with special needs, those still learning English and the honors students have opportunities to come together in dance, art, yearbook, chorus, band and sports. The “How was your day?” club meets five days a week after-school, a time during which teachers and top students are on hand to assist struggling ones with homework, class work, and social and emotional support.

The main school building was erected in 1911, and a new annex opened in 2002. The building has the capacity to hold about 1300 students.

The Learning Environment Survey shows an inconsistency in the perception of safety. Most students report that there is harassment, fighting and bullying among students at least some of the time, while most teachers report this is not happening.

Admissions: Zoned neighborhood school (Lydie Raschka, October 2013).
