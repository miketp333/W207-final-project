Our Insights

What’s Special

Creative and well-paced lessons, a history of inclusion

The Downside

Cramped classrooms, drab building

The NYC Lab Middle School for Collaborative Studies is among the most selective and sought-after middle schools in the city. It's a place where bright kids work hard and excel, but also have fun and express their creativity. It's a pioneer in the inclusion model of special education and a place where students actively work against homophobia and other injustices. The school has a stellar record of sending graduates to the city's most competitive high schools with more than half of its 8th-graders typically going on to specialized high schools.

The atmosphere is cozy and laid-back but lessons are nicely paced, with little wasted time. Teachers deliver lessons in a conversational tone and students seem to be comfortable enough to crack a joke or chat with teachers for a few moments at the end of the period.

Teachers get students quickly involved in lessons. In a 6th-grade humanities class, the teacher asked her students to define tricky words such as "statutes" and "multitudes," found in a poem from Shi Jing, the oldest existing collection of Chinese poetry. She then asked them to talk to a partner about the meaning of the poem. In an 8th-grade team-taught class, a teacher urged pairs of students to argue for the theme they had picked to write about (friendship, joy, acceptance or survival) from the novel Of Mice and Men.

All 6th-graders take the same math class after which they are tested and split into three different tracks: The top two groups take Regents-level algebra (about 150 out of 200 students), and the highest group goes beyond what is needed for the Regents. "It's much deeper," said Megan Adams, a former assistant principal at Lab who became principal in 2007. "We try to cultivate a love of math." Homerooms mix students of different academic abilities, as do humanities and science classes.

Science is one of the school's strengths, according to the principal. Teachers skillfully use a mix of talk, experimentation and even silence to help kids make discoveries. A 6th-grade science teacher, in a white lab coat, said little, instead allowing kids to draw their own conclusions as an almond burned inside a clear chamber. It took an impressively long time. "Awesome. Almonds are a lot better than Cheetos," said a boy. "It's more fibrous," said another. "You can see why it's popular for hiking," the teacher added. "It's a seed. It has to store a lot of energy."

A popular computer coding course may eventually become a three-year program. Teachers also infuse technology into science using Google docs and Edmodo, a Facebook-like site that is private where kids can post labs and share resources and feedback.

Students receive four periods of art per week, twice as much as is required by the state. On our visit, teens were setting up cameras and cutting out backgrounds to work on animated films with jointed puppets.

Well over 30 children are packed into each classroom and depending on the child, this could be viewed as a drawback. Several students seemed confused during a life science activity in 7th grade about respiration, although the teacher circulated and asked questions to help them understand. Students are also good about helping each other.

For lunch, 7th- and 8th-graders can leave the building, but they need to stay within defined street boundaries. Sixth-graders stay on the premises for the school's lunch period, but have the option to go to the library or participate in a lunchtime club.

Founded as a 6-12 school, in 2007 Lab Middle School split from the high school known as the NYC Lab School for Collaborative Studies. Though the two schools maintain a common vision and share a building (along with the New York City Museum School), they have separate admissions policies. Most of Lab Middle is housed on the fourth floor. About 35 kids from Lab Middle attend the high school while roughly 60 percent attend specialized high schools. Other popular choices include Beacon, Bard, Millenium and Eleanor Roosevelt.

Special education: Teachers are experimenting with iPads and other technology to assist students who have trouble staying organized or using paper and pen. Two of the six homeroom sections on each grade are integrated co-teaching (ICT) classes. There are about 24 students per grade identified as needing special services, according to Adams.

Admissions: Only children from District 2 schools gain admission. Lab uses a formula that takes into account academics (60 percent), and non-academics (40 percent), such as attendance and the ability to work well with others. Prospective students must select the school as their first choice and attend a tour or an open house to be eligible. (Lydie Raschka, June 2014)
