Our Insights

What’s Special

Technology-infused oceanography programs, gifted classes, strong after school program

The Downside

Low parent involvement, low test scores

The Shell Bank School is remaking itself into an aquatic learning center and improving its reputation in Sheepshead Bay with hands-on, technology-enriched programs and a community-minded staff. The school's stately, red brick building sits across the street from Sheepshead Bay High School and a block from the Sheepshead Bay housing projects, where many Shell Bank students live. While the school has a test-in gifted program, staff is working hard to raise otherwise low reading and math scores.

With the help of a 2007 federal magnet grant, Shell Bank has created three interrelated oceanography-themed academies, all works in progress on the day of our visit as the school waited for a final piece of fish-farming equipment. In a large, bright lab in the Aquatic Academy, students monitor tanks filled with tilapia fish that feed nutrient-rich water to hydroponic planters. Students in the Technology Academy are building fish-farming robots that they'll ship to a working fish farm at Rutgers University. In the Legal Studies Academy, which boasts a wood-paneled mock courtroom, students study the environmental issues of Sheepshead and Jamaica bays and plan to lobby local officials to clean up nearby Plum Beach.

Spearheading the re-invented school is Principal Anne Tully who took over in December 2005 after the former principal abruptly retired amidst allegations that she ignored kids cheating on a state social studies exam. (See comments below). Tully instituted color-coded uniforms that match the blue, yellow or white walls of each academy's floor. Student-painted murals in deep blues and purples decorate the wide, soaring corridors, and bulletin boards display creative essays and science projects.

Tully also focused teachers on "differentiation," where students break up into small groups according to ability. "That's our strength," she said. With the exception of the Center for Intellectually Gifted (CIG) classes (one per grade in each academy) and two classes for kids whose performance is teetering on grade level, classes contain students of all achievement levels. Students learning to speak English -- about 90 when we visited -- are either placed in self-contained classes for English language learners only or get "pulled out" of class for instruction.

On the day of our visit, we saw very strong group work in nearly every class we visited. Teachers conferred with small groups of students working earnestly on math problems and science posters. In a special education class, students worked together to integrate literacy with social studies by writing questions for ancient oracles and short essays about their dreams. In many classes, kids talked easily with us, happy to explain their projects.

With the help of an enthusiastic, music-turned-technology teacher, students restored old photographs with Photoshop and created intricate light displays for holiday shows in the large auditorium. In the technology lab, a student showed off her PowerPoint presentation on Motown Records founder Berry Gordy Jr., complete with footnote links and a music CD she'd burned. The school spends significant money on fun, educational equipment like a poster-making machine, microscopes and the technology lab's music equipment that doubles as a DJ booth for school dances. Roughly 150 laptops are available for use in classrooms throughout all the academies.

Shell Bank's discipline problems have diminished greatly, said Parent Coordinator Loriann Binckes, a former school aide and ten-year veteran of the school. "It used to be like Rikers [Island] around here," she said. Local police officers, who teach classes on avoiding bullying and gangs, gave the school an award for reducing crime. Binckes said she and Tully "walked the projects" one summer to promote the school to families. Still, she said, parents could be more involved.

Shell Bank was on the state's list of failing schools for the low reading scores of its special education students and students learning English in 2007, but improved enough to come off the list for math. To lift student performance, Tully created a "data center," where student scores on the periodic assessments that the Department of Education requires schools to administer are posted on the walls for teachers' reference. Classroom doors display "thermometers" showing the math and reading progress of the class. "Our ESL kids made the most progress," Tully said.

A few kids complained to Insideschools about roaches and the lack of soap and toilet paper in the bathrooms. Ms. Tully said the school just got a full time janitor and the situation should improve.

Special Education: Shell Bank is accessible to kids who use wheelchairs and serves many students with physical handicaps. It offers SETSS (special education teacher support services), self-contained classes for students with special needs only and collaborative team taught (CTT) classes, where two teachers work with a group of special and general education students.

After school: About 300 kids participate in after school programs run by the Flatbush YMCA, including basketball, football, chess, karate and arts and crafts. During school breaks, Shell Bank students can attend the YMCA for free. The school also offers homework help, and students with qualifying grades can prepare for high school Regents exams in Earth Science and Living Environment -- once reserved only for CIG students.

Admission: Neighborhood school. Admission to the CIG program is determined by a combination of factors making up a "composite score"—the final 4th grade report card counts for 40 percent, state tests 40 percent, attendance ten percent and student performance measures ten percent. (Nicole LaRosa, February 2008; admissions updated January 2015)
