What’s Special

Individual learning plans for each student

The Downside

Uneven discipline

New Heights Middle School MS 722, founded in 2012, offers a "small, caring environment where teachers really get to know each child" and where students feel there is an open door policy, said founding principal Jessica Luciano. "We want to get to know our scholars, give them choices in their middle school experience and prepare them to compete in the world so they feel confident."

Students, referred to as scholars, wear neat forest green uniforms with a polo shiirt displaying the school logo, and khaki pants or skirts. [The slideshow on this page is from the school's website.] They are encouraged to have a voice in the school. To help the kids understand the school's core values such as citizenship, respect and optimism, they were asked to devise a school mascot in art class and decided it would be an eagle, the principal said. [Ativia Sandusky replaced Luciano as principal in 2015.] The parent-teacher conference at New Heights is led by scholars with students writing letters inviting parents and guardians to attend and then leading the discussion about their work. "They really took leadership," said Michael Wiebusch, a social studies teacher leader.

The staff develops an individual learning plan for each student. Technology is integrated into the daily life of the school and each scholar has a school email account and shared access to Google Docs. Scholars create projects and collaborate with their classmates, said Wiebusch. "It's a really great tool for collaboration," he said. Projects include creating "A to Z vocabulary books on ancient Egypt, defining different words and adding pictures and citations." Students use the school's website to download homework assignments or see their grades.

"It's a small school. I'm able to visit classrooms and kids share Google Docs with me - they feel that I am accessible to them," said the principal who became a teacher with Teach for America and was for five years an assistant principal at the Dual Language Middle School in Manhattan.

Students visit nearby Prospect Park zoo and the Brooklyn Botanic Garden where they take classes aligned with what they are studying at school.

Incoming students attend a Student Summer Academy to allow the staff and students to get to know one another. Attendance in the school's first year was above the city average for middle school. "We were relentless," said the principal. "We made follow up phone calls, the parent coordinator reached out to families, and the guidance counselor reached out to those whose attendance was below 90 percent. And we celebrated kids' success."

New Heights occupies the first floor in a building shared with Achievement First Crown Heights Charter School.New Heights replaces the Middle School for the Arts which is closed in 2014 after years of poor performance. The building has a gym, auditorium and school yard.

A downside: Only half of the teachers responding to the 2015-16 school survey said order and discipline are maintained at the school, and just 57 percent said they would recommend the school to parents.

After school: Through PENCIL, an organization that matches business volunteers with public school principals, the school teamed up with SNHETTA, an architectural landscape and design firm that organized a gardening club. Sixth-graders got involved in hands-on math and science projects including designing a greenhouse in the science room. Other after school activiites include an art club, dance club, tech team, choir, boys basketball, flag football and drumming.

Special education: About 25 percent of the students have special needs. They are served in both team-teaching classes and smaller self-contained classes. They also get SETTS

Admissions: Zoned students and District 17 applicants who attend an information session, open house or fair. (Pamela Wheaton, interviews & web reports, June 2013, updated July 2017 to reflect new principal, discipline problems.)
