Our Insights

What’s Special

Arts program with animation, visual arts and band

The Downside

Overcrowded; friction between administration and staff

Opened in September 2000 to relieve overcrowding in nearby IS 145, MS 230, built to house 700 students, is today itself so overcrowded that it operates on double sessions and must hold four lunch periods a day. Despite this, the school is calm, as evidenced by the relatively quiet changes of class we saw, when Principal Sharon Terry urged stragglers to hurry--and they listened.

Terry became principal in 2004, after graduating from the Leadership Academy, the city's training ground for new principals. She apparently walked into an already well managed school. The school's founding principal, John Bongino, who had been an assistant principal at IS 145, was a "master of organization" who put in place the school's basic structures, according to Assistant Principal Ron Zirin. This organization has freed up Terry to focus on improving instruction, and she spends a good part of her day in classrooms, Zirin said.

In most of the classes we saw, students were engaged in and excited about their work. We were impressed by 6th grade science students who were investigating force by conducting experiments involving model cars. The kids discussed their work animatedly as their teacher moved from group to group. In an English-as-second-language class, children used computers to write a paragraph about themselves and then read it to their partners.

We also saw some room for improvement, particularly when it came to including student voices in lessons. The walls of an 8th grade English class, for example, were covered with teacher-written rules of composition, rather than examples of students' work. In the auditorium, 6th graders sitting in rows were working with partners to identify notes on paper lined with music staffs. The children were quiet and seemed to be following directions, but there was no singing of the notes, just writing them.

In 2006, the administration divided the school into four academies, each stressing a different interest, and now, 5th graders planning to enroll at MS 230 for 6th grade rank their preferences among the programs. Students in the Law and Government Academy study the court system, hold debates, participate in the Model United Nations program, and visit Washington, D.C., Albany, and Philadelphia. In the Communications, Arts and Drama Academy, students write and perform their own screenplays and collaborate with the Roundabout Theater Company and the American Museum of the Moving Image. Students in the Business and Finance Academy run the school store, trade on a simulated stock market, and write business proposals. The Environmental Health and Science Academy promotes Earth Day activities and runs a recycling program for the entire school.

In addition to taking the required academic subjects, half the students receive instruction during the year in music, art, or computers. The school has a well stocked art room, complete with a ceramics kiln and darkroom for photography classes. There are also two science labs, a computer lab, and a library where all classes receive regularly scheduled instruction.

We were impressed by the school's welcoming atmosphere. Entering the wide lobby, the visitor is struck by the couch near the entrance, the shelf filled with books for the taking outside the main office, and students' paintings lining the walls. During our tour, Terry was warm and professional to every staff member we passed. Students in the classrooms and in the corridors did not horse around and everyone appeared to be involved in a productive activity. Students are expected to wear a uniform, and most do. Students who engage in inappropriate behavior are judged by a jury of their peers and are offered community service instead of detention. Helping the staff clean up the lunchroom is the option students choose most, resulting in a clean, inviting space.

With so many immigrant students--about 60 percent of students are Hispanic and about 30 percent come from Asian countries--the school's English language instruction team may have its hands full. There is one faculty member who teaches a fulltime ESL class and three who work individually or in small groups with students in their regular classrooms.

Special education: The school has small "self-contained" classes for students with special needs; one teacher and an aide work in a class capped at 12 students. It also offers physical therapy.

After school: Tuesdays and Thursdays are devoted to tutoring. On Mondays and Wednesdays, students may participate in a school newspaper, karate, dance, music videos, photography, chess, and a business club. (Ellen Hausknecht and Dorothy Wilner, December 2006)
