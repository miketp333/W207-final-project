Our Insights

What’s Special

The school is very diverse, with immigrant students from all over the world.

The Downside

Inconsistent academic achievement.

PS/MS 279 is a warm, caring K–8 school, named after a local military pilot who died in the Persian Gulf War. The school is home to a diverse array of immigrants from countries all around the world, including Bangladesh, Latin America, the Caribbean, and West Africa. More than half of its students are learning to speak English.


The school’s test scores have been up and down in recent years, but Principal James Waslawski says he and his teaching staff are confronting the issue head on, and are willing to do whatever it takes to help students learn. For example, in English language arts classes in the middle school, students are separated into units based not on grade but on ability level in English, so that all the very recent immigrants in sixth, seventh and eighth grades are getting targeted help on reading and writing, while more advanced students are working on more challenging assignments. When a sixth-grade class did poorly across the board, the school reorganized to bring in all new sixth-grade teachers. Students who need extra help are often pulled into intensive classrooms with just a handful of peers.


We visited classrooms for every grade level and found the teaching to be strong and the students engaged. Waslawski says that teachers work hard to give students a context for what they are expected to do. We observed one teacher showing students how to write an analytic essay by choosing important details from an article; other teachers were talking through math problems while letting the students make the important connections on their own. Eighth-grade students excitedly told us about their hands-on science experiments, including one in which they remove a strawberry’s DNA.


The school has been featured in the news for its physical education program, which is designed to combat childhood obesity, a problem in the neighborhood. Students who participate come to school early for sports and exercise, and they stay after school twice a week for more.
It also has a partnership with Chess-in-the-Schools, a nonprofit organization that comes in to teach chess to students during and after school. Students who participate compete in local and national competitions. Chess-in-the-Schools recruits eighth-graders to participate in high school in its College Bound program, where they can receive academic tutoring, college prep and tours, help finding college scholarships, even employment opportunities.

The school’s kindergarten is located in an annex three blocks north of the main school building.


Special Education: PS/MS 279 offers a variety of classroom settings for students with IEPs, including classes of 12 students with a special education teacher plus a paraprofessional, and smaller “intervention” classes for groups of less than 10.


Admissions: PS/MS 279 is a zoned school. Most students begin in K or 1st grade and continue through the end of 8th grade. The school admits students to the upper grades as well.
(Tom Jacobs, February 2011)
