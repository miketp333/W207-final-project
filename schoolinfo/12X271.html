Our Insights

What’s Special

Computers, cameras and other technology make it fun to learn

The Downside

Rough neighborhood, many graduates not prepared for college

At East Bronx Academy for the Future, laptop computers, digital cameras, and software like Apple's GarageBand are all part of the students' daily tools for learning. Younger students make digital documentaries about their own lives, while older students may use technology to analyze poll results during an election year. The school serves children in grades 6-12.

Most students begin school performing below grade level, but by the time they get to high school they are doing pretty well and about three-quarters graduate on time, school officials said. Experienced teachers, 70-minute classes, and lots of lessons based on technology engage the students and improve their academic skills. Some classes combine on-line learning with a classroom teacher in what is called blended learning.

Founded in 2004 by Principal Sarah Scrogin, East Bronx Academy is housed in contemporary building located in a congested, commercial neighborhood. Elevated train tracks pass within feet of the school's soundproof windows. Inside, modern science labs and clean, brightly-lit hallways make for a pleasant learning space. Facilities include a library, dining hall, and multi-purpose room where physical education is held. There is no gymnasium. The school does not share the building with any other schools.

Even though the neighborhood is rough, kids say they feel safe inside the building, according to the Learning Environment Survey. Teachers say the problems in the neighborhood sometimes spill into the school, and some complain of bad behavior and drug use by students, according to the survey.

A downside: while the school does a good job graduating kids on time, many graduates need to take remedial courses when they get to college.

Special education: About 20 percent of the students receive special education services. The school offers team teaching classes as well as self-contained classes.

After school: The Salvadori Center, a not-for-profit organization that teaches principles of architecture and engineering through "real world" construction projects, offers an after-school program.

Admissions: District 12 choice for middle school, limited unscreened for high school. (Clara Hemphill, school website, interviews and statistics, July 2012)
