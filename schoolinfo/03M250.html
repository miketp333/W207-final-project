Our Insights

What’s Special

Lots of small group work; close connection with families

The Downside

Test scores have a ways to go

West Side Collaborative is a close-knit community with energetic teachers who have found creative ways to challenge high-achievers as well as ways to support those who struggle. This tiny middle school located on the top floor of PS 75 offers families an unusual amount of attention and guidance. Hallmarks are student-led conferences, small classes and a smart use of technology. 

West Side boasts an excellent attendance rate and very high satisfaction among staff, students and parents on the Learning Environment Survey. “It’s a small school but it’s very, very rich,” said a parent of four who has two children at the school. “What I love about this place is there is a real connection with the teachers. They know who you are.”

The school’s low test scores reflect, in part, the large proportion of students who receive special education services or who speak Spanish at home. However, West Side Collaborative has recently begun to attract strong students, including those who score Level 4 on state math and reading tests. The school offers a Regent’s prep algebra class to those who are ready for the challenge. The Department of Education has recognized the school for its success in preparing students well for high school.

Classes are never larger than 23 and these are frequently split into groups of four or six with extra adults in the room to assist. Teachers take turns working with students with special needs and these children are placed in smaller groups. Mixed-age groups of 13 children gather weekly with an adult to talk about feelings and identify passions and learning styles. “Base camp” is a small academic coaching program. About three weeks before report cards go home, students receive a “draft” report card and, in the interim, with help from the coach, they have a chance to catch-up and possibly boost their grade. “It’s a great way of holding kids accountable,” said a parent.

Instead of traditional parent-teacher conferences, the school offers student-led conferences in which children discuss their progress with their parents. Teachers are on hand to make sure these meetings are productive and parents may sign up to speak to teachers afterwards if they wish. 

Longtime principal Jeanne Rotunda encourages her teachers to share their expertise with one another. Staffers are unusually computer savvy thanks to a partnership with iZone, a technology program: step-by-step, self-paced projects are the norm. “Projects are a lot more free here,” said 8th grader Liam, who produced a movie about Abraham Lincoln for a history assignment. [Principal Jeanne Rotunda retired in 2014. She was replaced by Novella Bailey, who joined West Side in 2000 as a humanities and performing arts teacher and has served as a literacy leader at the school.]

All students complete four to five big projects each year, producing math and language portfolios along with a West Side "process portfolio." A 6th-grader clicked through her portfolio to show us a Power Point she made about different types of bridges; an opinion piece about child abuse; her goal sheet with steps to improve reading (“Read 11 pages a night”); an essay about the time she was bitten by a dog; and her “things I love” page with an image of the pink breast cancer awareness ribbon next to a picture of the pop boy band One Direction.

In the spring the entire school embarks on a weeklong exploration of science: topics include Rockets, the Human Body, the Hudson River and Farm to Table. Teens visit green markets, zoos and aquariums, dissect animals, watch videos of real-life surgeries and build rockets and launch them in Riverside Park.

Sixth-graders are not allowed to go off campus for lunch but 7th and 8th graders may earn the privilege and about 80 percent do so. Beyond academics students enjoy after-school sports, clubs including knitting, debate, band and an arts program run by Studio in a School.

Graduates from WSC attend one of up to 23 different high schools including Frank McCourt, LaGuardia, the NYCiSchool, Talented Unlimited, Beacon and Manhattan Village. 

Admissions: The school draws families from across the District 3 who mostly hear about it by word of mouth from happy alums. Prospective students should be prepared to show and talk about their work in small groups. “We look for how connected they are to the work that they are doing,” said Rotunda. (Lydie Raschka, October 2013; updated December 2014)
