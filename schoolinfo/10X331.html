Our Insights

What’s Special

Enthusiastic principal and teachers, hands-on curriculum.

The Downside

Test scores and student achievement have a way to go.

The well-maintained grounds and friendly atmosphere of MS 331 offer students a welcome haven in Morris Heights, a low-income neighborhood with many housing projects. The personality of the school is a stark contrast to many Bronx middle schools where transitions between classes are often bustling or even volatile. We saw students greeting each other in the halls and interacting with hearing impaired and wheelchair-bound peers with true companionship. Parent Coordinator Jolane Toro, whose own children attend the school, says This is a nurturing environment where the students inspire me to come to work every day.

MS 331 was created in 2004 when the middle school grades of PS/MS 306 were eliminated due to poor performance on state exams. The school is dedicated to science inquiry, but raising student achievement in all areas is the goal of Principal Serapha Cruz, a former assistant principal at the school and one of its original founders. Since taking the helm in 2008, Cruz has hired two full-time guidance counselors and several new teachers who are committed to working extra hours to bring student work up to grade level. The result is that test scores have improved somewhat, but there is still much work to be done. Thirty percent of students receive special education services and a large portion of students enter the school reading on a 3rd or 4th grade level. Parent involvement remains low, says Toro, because many families are new immigrants and uneducated themselves. Cruz hopes close the gap through afterschool classes for parents, teacher collaboration and professional development through partnership with Fordham University.

During our visit we saw plenty of hands-on learning. We saw an 8th-grade class break up into partnered groups to debate the arguments for and against slavery. Some students were very clear on the task at hand, while others were a bit unsure. However, everyone was engaged. All classes were very orderly, filled with bins of books and supplies, and the school security guard said that fights between students or poor behavior is rarely an issue. Although nearly half of students said that bullying is a problem some of the time on the school's Learning Environment Survey, on the day of our visit we saw happy, caring kids.

Incentive programs help keep kids focused on their studies. There are super scholar rewards for those who maintain an 85 or better in five subjects; and kids with outstanding attendance go on special field trips where they learn about careers in business and the arts. Sixth-graders work with the nonprofit Citizen Schools New York until 6 pm where they engage in apprentice study with community leaders for 10 weeks and visit local colleges. To bolster the schools science tradition, 6th-graders have an intensive weather unit, 7th-graders take an anatomy-based elective, and 8th-graders dissect worms and frogs.

Most graduates continue on to Bronx area high schools. DreamYard is a popular destination for students with an arts focus, while boys in need of a structured atmosphere head to Eagle Academy for Young Men.

After school: Extended day academics and sports are available along with many clubs including guitar club, step dance and hip hop.

Special education: Most classes are general inclusion, with only two self-contained classes that blend with their gen ed peers for English and math, There is a District 75 program for the hearing impaired that also shares in the general inclusion classes.

Admissions: Neighborhood school. (Jacquie Wayans, February 2012)
