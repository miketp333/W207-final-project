The Downside

Arts, music, and vocational classes have been cut.

After a decade of declining academic performance, poor teacher retention, and an exodus of its best students to specialized middle schools, IS 68 in Canarsie has taken the first step toward regaining the status it once had as a magnet school for math and science.

"I needed to get the building under control," said Iris Zvi, who became principal in 2003, when the huge middle school was put on New York State's "schools in need of improvement" list.

Zvi placed cameras in the halls and locked outside gates to prevent what had become frequent intrusions by outsiders. But the real change has come in the organizational restructuring that divided the huge school into three separate "academies," grouping about four classes from each grade on a floor together. Each academy is administered by an assistant principal, patrolled by a security officer and dean in charge of behavior issues, and served by its own guidance counselor. Teachers, students, and administrators all agree that the restructuring has been a triumph.

"A 180 degree change," said one teacher. "The results are fantastic," agreed another long-time faculty member. "We need to continue it and fine-tune it, but the school has a completely different tone."

Academic achievement is still low, and discipline remains an issue. Some students complained to us 8th graders occasionally intimidate 6th graders. The new academy structure means 6th and 8th graders now have lunch together, so there are more opportunities for "senior bullying," the younger children said.

IS 68 has adopted the America's Choice instructional program for literacy, which emphasizes reading and writing workshops, and classroom routine. Students keep a writer's "sourcebook" in which they record their responses to newspaper articles, shared readings, and other students' essays and stories. But at least one seasoned English teacher worries that the America's Choice program doesn't provide students with the fundamental literacy skills they need, particularly for youngsters who are new to the country.

IS 68 has a gifted program, called Astral, in which 8th graders take high-school level math and make use of a brand new science lab. All classes visit one of two computer labs regularly and may use them before school as well. Students we talked to tended to cite math as their favorite subject.

On our visit the halls and classrooms were orderly, and even the cafeteria was relatively controlled. A large gym allows all classes at least two periods of exercise weekly and hosts a girls' and boys' basketball team. Sadly, extras like band, chorus, art, and shop have been discontinued, victims of both a shortage of teachers and ramped-up mandates for academics.

Admissions: Astral testing is done in lower grades by the Region. IS 68 teachers may recommend that students be included in or taken out of the program.

English as a Second Language: There is one "self-contained," mixed-grade class for non-native English speakers only. Once students complete a year in that class, additional English help is offered outside the classroom.

Special education: There are eight "self-contained" classes -- only for children with special needs. One 6th grade class and one 7th grade "inclusion" class combine children with special needs with general education students.

After school: Classes focus on test preparation and are offered in three-month cycles to the grade with an upcoming standardized state test. Recreational after-school activities are held at the school but run by the Beacon community program. (Elizabeth Kiem, February 2005)
