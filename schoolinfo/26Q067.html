Our Insights

What’s Special

Strong academics, technology and arts; nice outdoor facilities

The Downside

Large classes and a relatively new teaching staff

Even in District 26, where high performing schools are the norm, MS 67 puts a premium on having its students excel -- in academics, in the arts and interschool academic competitions. With a student body drawn largely from surrounding middle class communities, the school boasts high test scores and an impressive record of high school admissions.

MS 67 houses one class per grade of a District 26 gifted program. Other than that and special education classes, all students at MS 67 take the same level of academic classes. In 2006, the school eliminated the distinction between special progress -- or advanced -- classes and regular ones, putting all the students on the accelerated program that previously had been available only to high performing students. This means everyone takes Earth Science and the Algebra Regents classes in grade 8. (The curriculum for the district gifted program is even more accelerated.)

Although many students arrive at MS 67 as high achievers, principal Zoi McGrath said there had been concern about whether all of them could handle the load. Teachers provide academic support before, during and after school to help. "Kids bubble up. You just have to have an environment in which kids can bubble up," McGrath said. In 2011, the majority of 8th graders earned high school credits by passing a Regents exam.

The school does have self-contained special education classes and has introduced a collaborative team teaching class. Special education students have teachers for individual subjects rather than a single teacher.

Classes are large -- at the max, McGrath said -- and 80 percent of the teaching staff is under 30 owing to a large number of recent retirements by veteran teachers. Despite that, classes seem orderly, with students attentive and engaged. And McGrath said the influx of new teachers has had an upside, producing a staff that is more computer savvy and more likely to encourage small group work than previous teachers had been.

Beyond the basic academic subjects, students take either French or Spanish and can choose from eight additional courses, including traditional arts offerings, robotics and reading as a talent. All classes feature some writing, with arts students, for example, creating essays on Picasso's approach to art along with paintings modeled to some extent on his work.

In most classes, students listen to the teacher for about 10 minutes and then work individually or in small groups. In an English class focusing on nonfiction, students sat at desks grouped in clusters and discussed possible chapter headings for a book by chimpanzee expert Jane Goodall. In science, groups tried to balance "scales" made from yardsticks.

While there are ample computers available, the school offers no computer class because it found its students did not need one. The school also has a mock courtroom, arts facilities and lots of outside space, owing partly to Louis Pasteur Park next door, which students can use during lunch hour.

Parent involvement is high. They contribute to the school community in many ways, such as providing workshops for one another -- a recent one highlighted etiquette for students --- and are not reluctant to speak out on issues of concern.

Last year, more than half of MS 67 students went on to specialized high schools, with many more attending Townsend Harris and specialized programs.

Admissions: The school is zoned. Admission to the gifted and talented program is based on a combination of factors that make up a "composite score"the final 4th grade report card counts for 35 percent, state tests 35 percent, student performance indicators 20 percent and attendance ten percent.(Gail Robinson, December 2011; admissions updated January 2015)
